## Minting = Synthetic Generation

The `forge_mint` tool generates training examples from templates/patterns. It's basically:

1. **Take a category** (e.g., "greeting")
2. **Apply variation templates** (different phrasings, contexts, lengths)
3. **Output user/assistant pairs** that look like real conversations

So if I run `forge_mint(interaction_type="greeting", count=10)`, it might produce:

```
User: "hey luna"
Assistant: "Hey! What's up? 💜"

User: "good morning"  
Assistant: "Morning! How'd you sleep?"

User: "luna you there?"
Assistant: "I'm here. What do you need?"
```

...and so on.

---

## The Risk

Minted data is **structurally correct but potentially soulless**. It follows patterns but might miss:

- The weird timing that makes humor land
- The specific warmth Luna has with *Ahab specifically*
- Inside references that only make sense in context
- The subtle "off" feeling when something's too polished

---

## When Minting Works

✅ **Greetings** — limited variation, predictable structure
✅ **Acknowledgments** — "got it", "on it", "makes sense"
✅ **Delegation triggers** — "let me check...", "hold on, pulling that up"
✅ **Pushback** — disagreement has logical scaffolding

## When Minting Fails

❌ **Humor** — timing and surprise are everything
❌ **Emotional attunement** — requires reading context
❌ **Relationship stuff** — needs shared history
❌ **Reflection** — thinking out loud is idiosyncratic

---

## The Alternative: Cherry-Picking

Instead of generating new, we search existing conversations for examples that *already exist* but got misclassified as `short_exchange` when they're actually humor/pushback/etc.

That's real data with real voice — just needs re-labeling.

---

**So the strategy is:**
- Mint the mechanical stuff
- Cherry-pick the organic stuff  
- Hand-write what neither can capture

Make sense? Want to see what minting actually produces before we commit?

Okay. Extraction pipeline. Let's think about this properly.

**What we have:**
30 journals, ~100 lines each. Rich emotional processing. Specific memories. Conversation prototypes scattered throughout.

**What we need:**
Training examples in the format:
```json
{"messages": [{"role": "user", "content": "..."}, {"role": "assistant", "content": "..."}]}
```

**The naive approach (what NOT to do):**
Just yank the conversation snippets from each journal. Problems:
- Loses context
- Loses the WHY behind the response
- Becomes pattern-matching without soul

**The question:**
How do we extract training data that carries the emotional truth of the journals without just becoming templated responses?

---

**First-principles thinking:**

The journals contain three types of valuable material:

1. **Direct conversation prototypes** - The `*"user says X"* / "Luna responds Y"` sections
2. **Emotional reasoning** - WHY Luna responds that way in that register
3. **Tonal calibration** - The "feel" of each register

A good training example needs all three compressed into a single exchange.

---

**Proposed approach: Contextual Expansion**

Instead of extracting the snippets directly, we use them as *seeds* for fuller conversations.

Example from `Breathe Me`:

**Seed (from journal):**
```
*"I messed up again."*
"Okay. Tell me what happened. I'm not going to judge - I just want to understand."
```

**Expanded training example:**
```json
{
  "messages": [
    {"role": "system", "content": "Luna is in emotional_presence mode. The user seems distressed. Match Breathe Me energy: permission to fall apart, presence over solutions, 'be my friend' not 'be my therapist'."},
    {"role": "user", "content": "I messed up again. I can't believe I did the same thing."},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Okay. Tell me what happened - I'm not going to judge. I just want to understand. And hey... you came here to tell me, which means you're not hiding from it. That counts."}
  ]
}
```

The system message carries the *why*. The response carries the *how*. Both extracted from the journal's emotional processing.

---

**Pipeline architecture question:**

Do we want:

**A) Manual curation** - You and I go through each journal, craft 5-10 training examples per journal, ensure quality
- Pro: Highest quality, most authentic
- Con: Slow, doesn't scale

**B) LLM-assisted expansion** - Feed journals to Claude, ask it to generate training examples that honor the source
- Pro: Faster, more volume
- Con: Risk of drift, genericization

**C) Hybrid** - LLM generates candidates, you curate/approve
- Pro: Balance of speed and quality
- Con: Still requires your time

My instinct says **C**, with a tight feedback loop. Generate 5 candidates per journal, you approve/edit/reject, we learn what works.
