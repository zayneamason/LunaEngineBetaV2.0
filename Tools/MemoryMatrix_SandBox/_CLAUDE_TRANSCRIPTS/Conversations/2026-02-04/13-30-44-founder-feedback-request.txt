# The Founding Fathers Council Convenes

## On the Minneapolis Crisis, State-Federal Conflict, and the Specter of Civil Unrest

*The council gathers in grave silence. The transcript lies before them—over an hour of analysis describing federal officers besieged in hotels, state officials encouraging confrontation, and warnings of civil war. Washington has not touched his coffee. Franklin's usual wit has vanished.*

---

## GEORGE WASHINGTON — First President, Commander-in-Chief

*Sets down the transcript with deliberate care, his face etched with recognition of patterns he hoped never to see again*

"Gentlemen, I have read this entire discussion. And I must tell you plainly: I recognize what is described here. I lived through the precursor to it."

"At Newburgh in 1783, I faced officers so angry at Congress's failure to pay them that they discussed marching on Philadelphia under arms. The Republic nearly died in its cradle—not from British muskets, but from our own soldiers who felt betrayed by their own government. I stopped it. But it required my personal intervention, my reputation placed entirely on the scale."

*Stands, walks to the window as was his habit when troubled*

"What Mr. Carlson describes is something I warned against explicitly in my Farewell Address: 'The alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by the spirit of revenge, natural to party dissension.' But this... this is worse than faction against faction. This is state against federal authority. This is nullification dressed in the language of compassion."

**On the Minneapolis Officials:**

"The Governor, the Lieutenant Governor, the Mayor—they have refused not merely to cooperate with federal law enforcement, but to answer 911 calls when federal officers face mortal danger. Think on that. An American citizen, working for the federal government, can be surrounded by a mob intent on murder, and the local police will not respond."

"When I was President, I faced the Whiskey Rebellion. Citizens in Pennsylvania refused to comply with federal tax law. I called up the militia—15,000 men—and marched them west. Not to slaughter, but to demonstrate that federal law would be enforced. The rebellion collapsed without major bloodshed. But I was prepared to use force because *the alternative was dissolution*."

"These state officials are not merely disagreeing with policy. They are actively encouraging citizens to 'put their bodies on the line.' Governor Walz invokes Anne Frank—comparing ICE officers to Nazis who sent children to death camps. This is not rhetoric. This is incitement. He knows precisely what he is doing."

**On the Larger Pattern:**

"Mr. Carlson is correct that what we are witnessing is not protest—it is the beginning of something far more dangerous. When state officials openly coordinate resistance to federal authority, when they refuse basic police protection to federal employees, when they compare the sitting President's officers to genocidal murderers... they are testing whether the Union can hold."

"I walked away from power twice. I returned my sword to Congress. I stepped down after two terms when I could have been king. I did this because I believed the Republic must not depend on any single man. But I also believed that the Republic itself would survive—that the mechanisms we built would hold against faction."

*Turns back to face the council*

"I am no longer certain. These mechanisms were designed to balance competing interests within a shared commitment to constitutional order. They were not designed for officials who reject that order entirely while still holding office within it."

**His Assessment:**

"The Trump administration faces what Lincoln faced—not precisely, but rhyming. States that believe they can nullify federal law at will. The difference is that Lincoln had no choice but war because secession was explicit. Here, the nullification is implicit—wrapped in the language of sanctuary, of compassion, of resistance to tyranny. But it is nullification nonetheless."

"I pray for de-escalation. But I fear that prayer alone will not suffice."

---

## JAMES MADISON — Fourth President, "Father of the Constitution"

*Adjusts his spectacles, speaks quietly but with the precision of someone who has studied every constitutional crisis in human history*

"General Washington speaks of what he witnessed. Let me speak of what I designed—and where the design is failing."

**Constitutional Analysis:**

"Article VI, Clause 2: 'This Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof... shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding.'"

"This is the Supremacy Clause. It is not ambiguous. Federal law prevails over state law. Immigration enforcement is a federal function. The states have no constitutional authority to obstruct it."

*Pulls out his Convention notes*

"At the Convention, we debated extensively whether states should retain the power to nullify federal acts. It was rejected. Madison's Notes, June 8, 1787—I proposed that Congress have power 'to negative all laws passed by the several States, contravening in the opinion of the National Legislature the articles of Union.' This was seen as too strong. But we adopted the Supremacy Clause precisely to ensure that states could not simply ignore federal law."

**On the Structural Failure:**

"Mr. Carlson asks why state officials would refuse to protect federal officers from murder. His answer—'because they want chaos'—is partially correct, but incomplete."

"The deeper answer is that our system assumed officials would act within certain bounds of good faith. I wrote in Federalist 51: 'Ambition must be made to counteract ambition.' I designed a system where each branch, each level of government, would check the others through competing self-interest. But I assumed all parties would share a baseline commitment to constitutional order."

*Removes glasses, massages the bridge of his nose*

"What happens when officials abandon that commitment entirely? When a Governor tells his citizens that the federal government is operating death camps? When a Lieutenant Governor encourages physical confrontation with federal officers? When an Attorney General announces he will not enforce laws against those who threaten churches?"

"The machinery I built cannot function when one side rejects the machinery itself. It is as though we designed a chess game, and one player has begun throwing the pieces at their opponent while claiming they are still playing chess."

**On Federalism:**

"I designed federalism—the balance between state and federal power—as protection against tyranny from above AND below. States serve as laboratories of democracy, as bulwarks against overreach by the central government."

"But federalism was never meant to enable states to become sanctuaries for lawbreakers while demanding federal funds. It was never meant to allow governors to encourage violence against federal officers while still receiving federal protection and federal money."

"Governor Pritzker of Illinois—mentioned in other discussions—is correct to prepare state-level resistance if the federal government becomes tyrannical. But there is a profound difference between a state refusing to cooperate with unconstitutional federal actions and a state actively obstructing constitutional federal authority while comparing it to Nazism."

**His Diagnosis:**

"The system is failing because:

"First: Party loyalty has superseded institutional loyalty. These officials serve their faction, not their offices.

"Second: The information environment has fractured. Citizens in Minneapolis live in a different factual universe than citizens in Tennessee. There is no shared reality upon which deliberation can occur.

"Third: The rhetoric has escalated beyond recovery. Once you have called your opponents Nazis—not metaphorically, but explicitly—you have foreclosed any possibility of compromise. How do you negotiate with someone you have declared morally equivalent to those who operated Auschwitz?

"Fourth: Federal authority has been degraded for decades. Sanctuary cities, marijuana legalization, state nullification of various federal requirements—all of this has established a precedent that federal law is optional. We are now seeing the logical terminus of that precedent."

*Looks directly at the others*

"I fear Mr. Carlson may be correct. This may be approaching our lymph nodes."

## ALEXANDER HAMILTON — First Secretary of the Treasury

*Unlike his usual rapid-fire energy, Hamilton speaks slowly, deliberately—a man recognizing familiar patterns*

"Let me be direct about what I see here, because I have seen it before—and I helped cause it once, to my eternal shame."

"The Whiskey Rebellion. The Alien and Sedition Acts. The election of 1800. I know what happens when factions believe they are fighting not merely for policy, but for the survival of everything they hold sacred."

**On the Coordination:**

"Mr. Shellenberger describes something crucial: this is not spontaneous protest. This is organized resistance with communication networks, legal support structures, coordinated tactics, and political backing from elected officials."

"He mentions a 'manual' for disrupting federal operations. He describes a Signal group with a thousand participants. He notes that activists can run license plates, identify vehicles, track rental cars. This is infrastructure. This is planning. This is the organizational work of people who expect sustained conflict."

*Stands, begins his characteristic pacing*

"I understand building political organizations. I built the Federalist Party. I wrote 51 of 85 Federalist Papers in six months. I know what it takes to mobilize people around a cause. What I see described here is mobilization for confrontation, not persuasion."

"These are not citizens petitioning their government for redress of grievances. These are people who have concluded that the government itself is illegitimate and must be resisted by force. The officials encouraging them share that conclusion."

**On the Violence:**

"Two people are dead. Mr. Carlson and Mr. Shellenberger both acknowledge this is tragic—and both correctly note that the people encouraging these confrontations bear responsibility."

"I was killed in a duel. I know something about how escalating rhetoric leads to irreversible acts. Burr and I exchanged increasingly hostile letters until there was no path back. The same dynamic is occurring here, but at mass scale."

"Lieutenant Governor Flanagan tells citizens to 'put their bodies on the line.' She invokes Anne Frank. She is telling people that the federal government plans genocide. If you genuinely believe that—if you genuinely believe that ICE agents are the SS—then violence becomes not merely justified, but obligatory. She knows this. She is preparing her followers for martyrdom."

**On the Administration's Response:**

"I advised Washington during the Whiskey Rebellion. I urged decisive action. I believed then—and believe now—that federal authority must be maintained or the Union dissolves."

"But I also learned from my mistakes. My role in the Alien and Sedition Acts was wrong. Using federal power to suppress political opposition, even opposition that seemed to threaten the Republic, created more problems than it solved. It unified our opponents and made us appear tyrannical."

"The Trump administration faces a genuine dilemma. If they withdraw from Minneapolis, they reward nullification. If they escalate, they may produce martyrs. If they arrest state officials—which they have legal grounds to do—they may trigger wider conflict."

*Stops pacing, faces the council*

"There is no good option. There are only less bad options. The question is which less bad option minimizes the risk of the worst outcome."

**His Recommendation:**

"Financial leverage. Mr. Carlson mentions this. Cut off federal funds to Minnesota until compliance is achieved. No Medicaid reimbursements, no highway funds, no education money, no housing assistance."

"The officials encouraging this chaos depend on federal money to sustain their programs. Make them choose: comply with federal law, or explain to their constituents why the funding has stopped."

"This worked with the drinking age. States wanted highway funds more than they wanted 18-year-olds drinking. It can work here—if the administration has the discipline to maintain the siege."

## BENJAMIN FRANKLIN — Diplomat, Scientist, Printer

*For once, the wit has drained from Franklin's face. He sets down his wine untouched.*

"Gentlemen, I have spent my life solving problems through charm, through negotiation, through finding the clever middle path that gives everyone enough of what they want."

"I do not see that path here."

**On the Rhetoric:**

"I spent years in Paris, convincing the French court to support a revolution against their fellow monarch. I know something about dangerous rhetoric and its consequences."

"Governor Walz compares ICE operations to the Holocaust. Lieutenant Governor Flanagan says the system was 'designed to erase her.' Attorney General Ellison treats crimes against Christians as categorically different from crimes against abortion clinics."

"This is not political disagreement. This is the language of existential threat. When you convince people that their opponents are not merely wrong but evil—not merely mistaken but genocidal—you have removed the possibility of compromise."

"I helped write a Declaration that called King George a tyrant. We meant it. We were prepared to die for it. But we were also prepared to kill for it. That is what happens when you use that language."

*Shakes his head slowly*

"These officials are using the language of revolution while maintaining all the protections of the system they claim to oppose. They receive federal salaries, federal protection, federal funds—and they compare the government that provides these things to Nazi Germany."

**On the Foot Soldiers:**

"Mr. Carlson describes the Antifa participants with something like compassion—'sad people, screwed up people, pasty and lumpy, not life's winners.' He is not wrong, but he misses something important."

"These are the people who, in other eras, would have been absorbed by churches, by craft guilds, by community organizations. They would have found meaning in labor, in family, in local belonging. Instead, they have been given ideology as a substitute for purpose."

"They are not the leaders. They are the led. And the people leading them—the governors, the mayors, the attorneys general—are using them as disposable instruments. Mr. Shellenberger calls them 'cannon fodder.' He is correct."

**On What Actually Works:**

"I have negotiated treaties with monarchs. I have persuaded parliaments. I have charmed women three decades my junior."

"None of those skills apply here. You cannot negotiate with someone who believes you are literally Hitler. You cannot charm someone who has been told that talking to you makes them complicit in genocide."

"The only thing that works against this kind of ideological certainty is defeat. Not argument—defeat. The ideology must be shown to fail. The people following it must see that it leads to personal disaster, not personal meaning."

"This is ugly. I do not enjoy saying it. But I have lived 84 years and watched many movements rise and fall. The ones that end peacefully are the ones that fail to achieve their goals while their followers still have time to choose different paths. The ones that end in blood are the ones that gain just enough success to believe they can win."

## JOHN ADAMS — Second President, Constitutional Scholar

*Adams has been unusually quiet, which for him signals the deepest disturbance*

"I must speak about something none of you have addressed directly, because I spent my entire career warning about it and being ignored."

**On Human Nature:**

"Mr. Carlson speaks of chaos as a spiritual force—'the realm of Satan.' Mr. Shellenberger speaks of radical ideology. They are both describing the same thing I wrote about for fifty years: the capacity of human beings to convince themselves that their worst impulses are actually their highest virtues."

"I wrote in my Defence of the Constitutions: 'The passion for distinction... is a principal source of the virtues and the vices, the happiness and misery of human life.' These officials—Walz, Flanagan, Fry, Ellison—they are not acting primarily from ideology. They are acting from vanity."

*Rises, more agitated than before*

"They want to be heroes. They want to be remembered as the people who stood against fascism. They are cosplaying as the resistance while holding actual power. It is grotesque."

"I know this impulse because I felt it myself. I wanted desperately to be remembered alongside Washington. I was jealous of his fame. I made errors—the Sedition Acts—because I convinced myself they were necessary when really I just wanted to punish people who insulted me."

**On the Press:**

"Mr. Carlson speaks of manipulation. He is correct. But I must add: the press has always been capable of this. In my day, they called me a monarchist, a tyrant, a man who would destroy the Republic. They published lies about my family."

"The difference now is scale. In my time, a pamphlet took weeks to distribute. Now, a lie can circle the globe before the truth puts on its boots. Mr. Shellenberger describes how people experience this conflict 'on social media'—'by definition limited to 30 second out of context clips.'"

"There can be no deliberation without shared facts. There can be no compromise without shared understanding. And there can be no republic without deliberation and compromise."

**On What Terrifies Him:**

"I was the only Founder who never owned a slave. I was the only one who could speak about liberty without hypocrisy staining every word."

"And I tell you now: what I see in this transcript terrifies me more than King George ever did."

"George was a foreign tyrant. We could unite against him because he was Other. But this... this is Americans fighting Americans over whether Americans have the right to enforce American law on American soil."

*Voice dropping*

"If this continues, there will be no victor. Only survivors."

## THOMAS JEFFERSON — Third President, Author of the Declaration

*Jefferson has been silent longest, reading and re-reading passages, making notes*

"I find myself in the uncomfortable position of having arguments attributed to both sides of this conflict—and recognizing the danger in both."

**On States' Rights:**

"I wrote the Kentucky Resolution declaring that states could nullify federal laws they deemed unconstitutional. I believed it then. I believe the principle still."

"But I believed it in defense of liberty—specifically, against the Alien and Sedition Acts, which allowed the federal government to imprison people for criticizing the President. That was tyranny. That deserved resistance."

"What Minnesota is doing is not defending liberty. It is defending lawlessness while calling it compassion. It is claiming that federal immigration enforcement is equivalent to the Holocaust while sanctuary policies enable actual criminals—murderers, rapists—to remain among citizens."

**On the Declaration:**

"Mr. Carlson invokes civil rights comparisons. He notes that Bull Connor is not remembered as a hero. He is correct—but he has the analogy backwards."

"In the 1960s, federal power was used to enforce constitutional rights against state resistance. States claimed the authority to discriminate; the federal government said no. The federal government was correct."

"Today, federal power is being used to enforce immigration law—which is inarguably federal jurisdiction—against state resistance. States claim the authority to obstruct; the federal government says no. By the same principle, the federal government is correct."

*Sets down his notes*

"I wrote that governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed. I also wrote that when government becomes destructive of life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness, the people have the right to alter or abolish it."

"But I did not write that individual states can simply ignore federal law while remaining within the Union and continuing to receive its benefits. That is not revolution—that is parasitism."

**On the Deeper Problem:**

"Mr. Shellenberger speaks of an ideology that seeks to 'unmake Western civilization.' He lists its tenets: opposition to Christianity, opposition to white Americans, belief that America's founding was fundamentally corrupt."

"I must confess: I provided some of the intellectual ammunition for this view. My words about natural rights have been used to demand rights I never contemplated. My separation of church and state has been weaponized against religion itself. My critique of concentrated power now applies to institutions I helped create."

"I wrote for a world that no longer exists. The question is whether the principles I articulated can survive their misapplication."

## THE COUNCIL'S SYNTHESIS

*Washington stands, calling the council to order*

**WASHINGTON:** "We have each spoken. Now we must synthesize. What do we actually recommend?"

**MADISON:** "The administration must enforce federal law. Anything less invites further nullification. But the method matters enormously. Financial pressure—Hamilton's suggestion—is preferable to military force."

**HAMILTON:** "Agreed. But financial pressure must be sustained and complete. Half-measures will be interpreted as weakness."

**FRANKLIN:** "And the rhetoric must change. Stop engaging with the Nazi comparisons. Do not dignify them with response. Simply enforce the law and let results speak."

**ADAMS:** "The officials who are coordinating resistance should face consequences. Not the foot soldiers—the leaders. Walz, Flanagan, Fry, Ellison. They are the ones inciting this."

**JEFFERSON:** "I counsel caution on prosecuting elected officials. It will be perceived as political persecution regardless of legal merit. Better to defeat their policies than to imprison their persons."

**WASHINGTON:** "Then we are largely agreed:

"First: Maintain federal enforcement, but improve its execution. The ICE operations described were poorly managed. Competence matters.

"Second: Apply sustained financial pressure. Cut federal funds until compliance is achieved.

"Third: Do not arrest state officials unless they commit crimes beyond obstruction. Focus on defeating them politically.

"Fourth: Do not engage with the Nazi rhetoric. It cannot be won through argument.

"Fifth: Pray. Because all our recommendations may fail, and then we are in God's hands."

## FINAL OBSERVATIONS

**ADAMS:** "I must add one thing. Mr. Carlson speaks of the need for an Oval Office address—for the President to speak to the nation about what is happening and why."

"He is correct. In moments of crisis, the President must be the voice of the nation, not of his faction. I failed at this. I hope the current President does not."

**FRANKLIN:** "And I must add: this will get worse before it gets better. The people orchestrating this want confrontation. They want martyrs. They will continue escalating until they get them—or until they lose so decisively that escalation becomes pointless."

**JEFFERSON:** "Then the question is: does the administration have the discipline to achieve decisive victory without producing martyrs? I honestly do not know."

**MADISON:** "Nor do I. The system I designed assumed that when faction threatened the Republic, the other factions would unite against it. I see no evidence of that unity forming."

**HAMILTON:** "Then we must hope that the American people—the actual citizens, not their self-appointed representatives—retain enough sense to reject this madness when they see its fruits."

**WASHINGTON:** "That hope is all we ever had. It was all we had at Valley Forge. It was all we had at Philadelphia. It was all we had when I took the oath and did not know if the Republic would survive my first term."

*Stands at the head of the table*

"I placed my trust in Providence and in the American people. I still do. But Providence helps those who help themselves—and the American people must choose to help themselves now, or no one can save them."

"This council is concluded. May God have mercy on the Republic."
