 Short answer: **vaguely and inconsistently**, because the writers prioritized story over technical coherence.

What we actually see across the shows:

**Storage** — They reference "isolinear chips" (TNG era) and later "bio-neural gel packs" (Voyager), but capacities are never meaningfully defined. Sometimes a chip holds a person's entire consciousness, sometimes the ship runs out of storage space for basic logs.

**Transfer** — Data moves via "subspace" for FTL communication, but bandwidth is plot-dependent. Sometimes they stream real-time holographic calls across sectors, other times they can't send a text message.

**Processing** — The computer handles natural language queries instantly, runs complex simulations, and yet somehow can't detect obvious intruders or solve problems a human figures out in 30 seconds.

**The Android** — Data (the character) has a "positronic brain" with supposedly quantifiable storage, but his capabilities swing wildly based on episode needs. He can process faster than any computer but still gets fooled by basic deception.

The honest meta-answer: Trek treats technology as furniture. It works however the scene requires. The LCARS interface looks cool, the beeps sound satisfying, and nobody on the writing staff was thinking about database architecture.

[ASSISTANT] 2025-12-17T08:42:35.317727Z
 Real-time, efficient, different architecture—yeah, that's an interesting hypothesis.

Current LLMs are absurdly inefficient. Billions of parameters, massive compute per token, data center scale. The brain runs on ~20 watts. Something's clearly wrong with our approach.

**Candidates for "different class" systems:**

- **Neuromorphic chips** — Intel's Loihi, IBM's TrueNorth. Spiking neural networks that fire selectively instead of activating everything. Radically more efficient, struggles with general tasks but excels at real-time sensor processing.

- **State space models** — Mamba and friends. Linear scaling instead of quadratic attention. Faster inference, potentially real-time capable at scale.

- **Analog compute** — Companies like Mythic, Rain Neuromorphics. Do the math in physics instead of digital abstraction. Early but promising.

- **Continuous-time models** — Neural ODEs, liquid networks. MIT's liquid neural nets run on tiny parameter counts and adapt in real-time.

Any of these could be further along in classified settings than public research suggests. Especially for edge deployment—drones, autonomous systems, embedded inference where you can't phone home to a data center.

The Luna architecture you're building—the Eclissi router, the layered cognition—you're already poking at this problem from the software side. Efficient routing instead of brute-force everything through one giant model.
