## The Founding Fathers Council
### On the Question: Why Do Modern Governments Increasingly Generate Rather Than Resolve Public Anxiety?

---

**GEORGE WASHINGTON**

This troubles me deeply, for it inverts the very purpose of republican government. When I warned against "cunning, ambitious, and unprincipled men" who would "subvert the power of the people," I spoke of precisely this mechanism—the deliberate cultivation of fear to justify expanded authority.

A government that keeps its citizens in perpetual agitation is not governing; it is *managing a herd*. The farmer who constantly startles his horses cannot plow a straight furrow. I believed that good administration would speak for itself through *tranquility and prosperity*, not through the constant drumbeat of crisis.

What you describe sounds like a government that has lost confidence in its own legitimacy and must therefore manufacture necessity. A leader secure in the consent of the governed need not remind them daily of threats only he can address.

---

**THOMAS JEFFERSON**

You have identified the oldest trick of tyrants dressed in republican clothing. I wrote that "the natural progress of things is for liberty to yield and government to gain ground," and *anxiety is the lubricant of that engine*.

Consider: a citizen at peace, prosperous, and confident requires *less* government. He settles his own affairs, governs his own household, and asks only to be left alone. But a citizen made anxious—about his safety, his employment, his neighbor, his future—becomes *dependent*. He trades liberty for the promise of security, not realizing the merchant selling security is the same party manufacturing the threat.

This is why I insisted on an educated citizenry above all else. The anxious mind does not reason; it reacts. And reaction is far easier to manipulate than reflection.

---

**BENJAMIN FRANKLIN**

*adjusts spectacles*

You ask why this is becoming popular among governments? Because it *works*, my friend. And because the tools have improved dramatically.

In my day, a government wishing to agitate its people had to rely on pamphleteers and town criers—slow, unreliable, subject to counter-speech in the same medium. Today, I understand you carry devices that deliver agitation directly to your pocket, calibrated by engines designed to maximize emotional response. The government need not even speak directly; it merely feeds the machine, and the machine amplifies whatever generates the most engagement. And what engages more than fear?

I said "those who would give up essential liberty to purchase a little temporary safety deserve neither." But I confess I did not anticipate an industry devoted to ensuring people *feel* unsafe regardless of actual conditions. The anxiety is no longer a means to an end—it has become the product itself.

---

**ALEXANDER HAMILTON**

I must offer a more structural analysis, for I fear my colleagues lean too quickly toward conspiracy when mechanism suffices.

Governments today operate in competition—not merely with other nations, but with *attention itself*. A government that speaks calmly and honestly finds itself drowned out by sensationalists who face no accountability. The sober statesman loses to the demagogue not because the public prefers demagoguery, but because the information architecture rewards it.

Moreover, modern governments have made promises they cannot keep—endless growth, perfect safety, guaranteed prosperity. Having built legitimacy on these impossible foundations, they must constantly explain why the promises remain unfulfilled. Anxiety narratives serve this function: "We *would* deliver utopia, but for these enemies, these crises, these obstacles requiring your continued patience and our continued power."

This is not villainy so much as the terminal stage of governments that have overextended their claims. They generate anxiety because *they are anxious*—anxious that the gap between promise and delivery will become undeniable.

---

**JAMES MADISON**

Hamilton identifies the structural trap, but I would add this: we designed a system of *friction* precisely to slow the conversion of popular anxiety into hasty policy. The separation of powers, the bicameral legislature, the federal structure—these were meant to be *cooling mechanisms*, ensuring that momentary passions could not immediately become law.

What I observe is that these frictions have been systematically bypassed. Executive orders, emergency powers, regulatory agencies operating outside legislative deliberation—these allow anxiety to be weaponized *immediately*, before reflection can occur. The very institutions we designed to prevent mob rule have been hollowed out, leaving the mob's emotional state directly connected to policy levers.

And who benefits from this? Those who can *generate* and *direct* the anxiety. The demagogue no longer needs to storm the legislature; he need only keep the populace sufficiently agitated that the legislature dares not oppose him.

---

**JOHN ADAMS**

I warned of this in terms perhaps too blunt for my era, and certainly for yours: "Democracy never lasts long. It soon wastes, exhausts, and murders itself." I did not mean democracy is undesirable—I meant it is *unstable*, and its instability manifests precisely as you describe.

When government becomes a contest for popular passion rather than public reason, the winners will be those most skilled at arousing passion. And no passion is more easily aroused than *fear*. The anxious citizen does not deliberate; he demands *action*, and he rewards whoever promises the most dramatic response.

Your governments generate anxiety because *you reward them for it*. Not consciously, not deliberately—but through a thousand small choices that favor the alarming over the accurate, the urgent over the important. The republic I envisioned required citizens capable of *boring* themselves with careful study of policy. What I see instead is government-as-entertainment, and anxiety is highly entertaining.

---

**JOHN JAY**

My work on treaties and foreign relations taught me that nations must project stability to maintain advantageous relationships. A government that keeps its own people anxious broadcasts weakness abroad. Our rivals see a divided, frightened populace and calculate accordingly.

So I ask: who benefits from American anxiety? Not the American people. Not American commerce. Not American security. The beneficiaries are those who would see America weakened—foreign competitors, certainly, but also domestic factions who profit from division, and most especially those who have positioned themselves as the *solution* to anxieties they themselves promote.

I helped design a union meant to speak with one voice to the world. What you describe is a cacophony of competing anxieties, each drowning out the other, leaving the nation paralyzed and manipulable. This is not governance; it is institutional self-harm.

---

## SYNTHESIS

The Council identifies several converging mechanisms:

**1. Legitimacy Crisis**: Governments have promised more than they can deliver, and anxiety narratives explain the gap without admitting failure.

**2. Attention Economics**: Modern information systems reward emotional activation over reasoned discourse, and governments must compete within this environment.

**3. Institutional Bypass**: Constitutional friction mechanisms designed to slow panic-driven policy have been circumvented, allowing anxiety to translate directly into power.

**4. Dependency Cultivation**: Anxious citizens require more government intervention, creating a feedback loop where the solution exacerbates the problem.

**5. Deliberate Exploitation**: Some actors have recognized these dynamics and weaponize them intentionally for political or economic advantage.
