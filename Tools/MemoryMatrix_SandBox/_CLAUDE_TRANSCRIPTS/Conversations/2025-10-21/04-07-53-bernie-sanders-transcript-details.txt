 # Bernie Sanders's "No Kings" Rally Speech - Analysis

**October 18, 2025, Washington DC**

## Core Message

Senator Sanders delivered a speech at a massive protest rally opposing what he characterizes as President Trump's consolidation of power and move toward authoritarianism. The speech invokes the American Revolution's rejection of monarchy to frame contemporary political resistance.

## Main Themes

### 1. **Defense of Democracy Against Authoritarianism**
Sanders argues that Trump is threatening the "experiment" of American self-governance by:
- Claiming excessive executive power
- Deploying military forces domestically
- Threatening to arrest political opponents (NY Attorney General, California Governor, a US Senator)
- Intimidating media and undermining First Amendment
- Threatening judges who rule against him
- Ignoring Congressional appropriations
- Firing federal employees and breaking union contracts

### 2. **"No Kings" - Revolutionary Framing**
Sanders explicitly connects to 1776:
- The founders rejected King George III's absolute power
- They established "We the people will rule"
- 2025 rallies echo that message: "No, President Trump, we don't want you or any other king"

### 3. **Oligarchy Critique**
Sanders extends beyond Trump to target billionaire influence:
- Names specifically: Elon Musk, Jeff Bezos, Mark Zuckerberg
- Notes they sat behind Trump at inauguration
- Argues they've "hijacked our economy and political system"
- Highlights wealth inequality:
  - Musk owns more than bottom 52% of American households
  - Top 1% owns more than bottom 93%
  - 60% of Americans live paycheck to paycheck

### 4. **Healthcare Shutdown Crisis**
The immediate policy fight:
- **18-day government shutdown** ongoing
- Trump's bill cuts Medicaid and Affordable Care Act
- **15 million Americans** will lose healthcare
- Sanders claims **50,000 will die unnecessarily** per year
- **20 million** will see premiums double
- **Purpose**: To fund **$1 trillion in tax breaks** for the wealthy

Sanders's red line: "I will not vote for a budget that throws 15 million Americans off their healthcare"

### 5. **Progressive Policy Vision**
Sanders lists what's possible "when we stand together":
- End Citizens United / stop billionaires buying elections
- Universal healthcare as a human right
- Affordable housing for young people
- Tuition-free public colleges
- Best childcare/schools in the world
- Expand Social Security
- $15+ minimum wage / union rights
- Green energy transformation
- Abortion rights
- End US support for "starving children in Gaza"

**Funding mechanism**: Tax the wealthy and corporations

### 6. **Historical Optimism**
Sanders invokes past victories when people were told change was "impossible":
- Founders defeating King George
- Abolitionists ending slavery
- Workers forming unions
- Women winning the vote
- Civil Rights movement ending segregation
- LGBT rights victories

**Message**: "They did it then. We can and will do it now."

## The Rally Context

Sanders emphasizes this isn't just a DC event:
- **"Thousands of rallies"** nationwide simultaneously
- **"More people out on the streets... than we have ever seen in American history"**
- Republicans called these "Hate America events"
- Sanders counters: "We love America" - defending democracy is patriotic

## Rhetorical Strategy

1. **Revolutionary legitimacy**: Frames resistance as continuing the founders' work
2. **Moral urgency**: 50,000 deaths/year = life-or-death stakes
3. **Class warfare**: Billionaires vs. working people
4. **Historical inevitability**: Previous movements won against long odds
5. **Unity appeal**: "Don't allow Trump... to divide us up by the color of our skin or where we were born"

## Key Quote

> "The establishment... want you to believe that you are powerless. They want you to believe that you cannot change the status quo. But that is a lie."

## What's Notable

- Sanders is treating October 2025 as a constitutional crisis moment
- The healthcare/shutdown issue is his immediate mobilization point
- He's explicitly naming billionaires as the real threat beyond Trump
- The speech assumes Trump has been taking authoritarian actions for months
- Mass protests are characterized as historically unprecedented in scale

This is Sanders in full revolutionary mode - invoking 1776, warning of oligarchy, rallying mass resistance, and promising nothing less than preserving American democracy itself.

 # The Founding Fathers' Council: Response to Bernie Sanders's Speech

## The Context They're Reviewing

The council examines Senator Sanders's October 2025 speech warning of executive overreach, oligarchic capture, and threats to constitutional governance—delivered at what he describes as the largest protest movement in American history.

---

## **George Washington** - First President, Commander-in-Chief

**His Immediate Concern: The Precedent of Military Deployment**

Washington would be deeply troubled by Sanders's claim that Trump "calls in the US military" for domestic protests and threatens to arrest governors and mayors who resist.

From his experience, Washington knew the danger of military power in civilian affairs. He deliberately resigned his commission in 1783 and walked away from power in 1797 precisely to establish that **the military serves the Constitution, not the president personally**.

The image Sanders paints—"masked agents working for ICE, breaking down doors, throwing people into vans without due process"—would alarm him greatly. This resembles the very tyranny Americans fought against: soldiers enforcing executive will without legal process.

**However**, Washington would also question the rhetoric. He spent his presidency trying to calm partisan passion, not inflame it. He'd want to know: Are these claims documented? Is this speech unifying the country or deepening division? Washington believed that **faction and party spirit** were the greatest threats to the republic. He'd worry that Sanders's populist framing—"billionaires vs. the people"—risks the kind of class warfare that destabilizes nations.

His likely counsel: "If the president violates his oath, Congress must act through constitutional means—impeachment, legislation, appropriations. But rallies in the streets? That's how republics collapse into mob rule. Address this through institutions, not mass movements."

**Washington's fundamental question**: "Does this strengthen the rule of law, or replace one form of dangerous passion with another?"

---

## **Thomas Jefferson** - Author of the Declaration of Independence

**His Reaction: Recognition and Deep Conflict**

Jefferson would hear echoes of his own revolutionary rhetoric in Sanders's speech. The "No Kings" framing, the invocation of 1776, the emphasis on popular sovereignty over executive power—**this is Jefferson's language**.

He wrote that "a little rebellion now and then is a good thing" and believed the tree of liberty must be refreshed periodically. Sanders's description of millions taking to the streets would not alarm Jefferson—**it would validate him**. The people ARE supposed to check power when it becomes tyrannical.

Jefferson would nod vigorously at Sanders's critique of oligarchy. He spent his entire political career fighting Hamilton's vision of government serving wealthy commercial interests. The idea that Elon Musk, Jeff Bezos, and Mark Zuckerberg sat behind Trump at inauguration—**literally positioned as the new aristocracy**—would confirm Jefferson's worst fears about concentrated wealth corrupting republicanism.

**But here's Jefferson's internal contradiction**: He'd struggle with Sanders's policy agenda. Universal healthcare as a "human right"? Massive federal social programs? This is exactly the centralized governmental power Jefferson spent his life opposing. He believed in small government, states' rights, and individual liberty—not federal guarantees of economic security.

Jefferson would find himself in an impossible position: **He agrees with Sanders's diagnosis (oligarchy threatening democracy) but would oppose Sanders's prescription (expansive federal programs funded by wealth redistribution)**.

His likely statement: "The concentration of wealth Sanders describes is indeed a mortal threat to our republic. But the solution is not to enlarge federal power further—that only gives these oligarchs a bigger prize to capture. We must return power to states and communities, break up concentrations of both economic AND political power, and trust in an educated citizenry to govern themselves locally."

**Jefferson's dilemma**: How do you fight oligarchy without creating the very centralized power that oligarchs will eventually control?

---

## **Benjamin Franklin** - Diplomat, Inventor, Pragmatist

**His Response: "Show Me the Numbers, Then Let's Deal"**

Franklin would be the most pragmatic voice in the room. Forget the revolutionary rhetoric for a moment—**are Sanders's claims about healthcare accurate?**

Franklin spent his life solving practical problems. He'd want to see:
- The actual bill text
- The CBO analysis
- Whether 15 million losing coverage is verified
- Whether 50,000 deaths/year is a credible estimate
- What the tax cuts actually say

**If the numbers check out**, Franklin would be furious. He believed government should serve the common welfare. His Pennsylvania Gazette championed practical solutions to public problems. The idea of cutting healthcare for 15 million people to fund tax breaks for billionaires would strike him as both **morally bankrupt and economically stupid**.

Franklin understood that healthy workers are productive workers. Sick people can't contribute to society. From a pure utility standpoint, this trade-off makes no sense.

But Franklin would also laugh at Sanders's revolutionary fervor. "Bernie, my friend, you're 83 years old, you've been in Congress for decades, and you're leading a revolution against *yourself*? You ARE the establishment!"

**Franklin's approach would be transactional**: "Here's what we do. The healthcare cuts are morally indefensible and economically foolish—oppose them absolutely. On executive overreach, document every violation, take it to court, and make the legal case systematically. On the oligarchy problem, that's real—but your solution of massive taxation will drive capital and innovation elsewhere. We need smarter approaches: break up monopolies, enforce antitrust, create competition."

**Franklin's bottom line**: "Fix the immediate healthcare crisis. Address executive overreach through law, not rallies. And for God's sake, Bernie, be more fun about it. You're making revolution look exhausting."

---

## **James Madison** - Father of the Constitution

**His Analysis: Structural Failure, Not Individual Villainy**

Madison would read Sanders's speech and think: **"The system is working exactly as I feared it would when checks and balances fail."**

Everything Sanders describes—executive overreach, Congress unable to constrain the president, oligarchic capture, faction overwhelming common good—Madison **warned about this explicitly** in Federalist #10 and #51.

**His diagnosis**: This isn't about Trump being a would-be king. This is about **institutional decay**:

1. **Congress has abdicated its power** - If the president is ignoring appropriations and Congress does nothing, that's Congress's failure, not just Trump's overreach. Where is impeachment? Where are the purse strings being pulled? Congress has been surrendering power to the executive for decades.

2. **The party system has failed** - Madison's constitutional design assumed that **ambition would counteract ambition** across branches. But when one party controls both Congress and the presidency, that check disappears. Republicans won't constrain Trump because party loyalty trumps institutional loyalty.

3. **Faction has triumphed** - The billionaire oligarchy Sanders describes is exactly what Madison meant by "faction"—a group united by interest adverse to the common good. Madison thought a large republic would prevent any single faction from dominating. He was wrong. Concentrated wealth found a way.

**Madison's frustration**: "I designed a system where power checks power. But if Congress won't use its powers, if courts are threatened, if parties value victory over constitutional duty, **no document can save you**. The Constitution is only as strong as the people who uphold it."

**On Sanders's mass protests**: Madison would be ambivalent. He believed in popular sovereignty, but he also feared majority tyranny. Mass movements can defend democracy—or they can become mobs that destroy it. Everything depends on whether they're channeling popular will through constitutional mechanisms or replacing institutions with street power.

**Madison's prescription**: 
- **Congress must reassert itself** - Impeachment, appropriations, oversight
- **Courts must check executive power** - But they need congressional support when threatened
- **Constitutional amendments may be necessary** - If the system is this broken, perhaps formal changes are needed
- **Break up factions** - Antitrust enforcement, campaign finance reform, anything to prevent oligarchic capture

**Madison's warning**: "Bernie is right that this is a constitutional crisis. But speeches won't fix it. Congress must act, or the republic will fall—not to Trump specifically, but to the structural rot that made Trump possible."

---

## **John Adams** - Second President, Anxious Defender of Institutions

**His Reaction: "I TOLD YOU THIS WOULD HAPPEN!"**

Adams would be simultaneously vindicated and furious. He spent his entire career warning about exactly this scenario—demagogues, mob politics, and the fragility of republican government.

**What would resonate**: Sanders's description of Trump undermining institutions, attacking the press, threatening judges, and demanding personal loyalty. Adams **obsessed** over the danger of executives becoming monarchs. His Alien and Sedition Acts (which Jefferson and Madison opposed through the Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions) were his misguided attempt to protect government from what he saw as seditious attacks.

Adams would see bitter irony: **He was accused of monarchical tendencies for far less than what Sanders describes Trump doing**. Adams never threatened to arrest governors. He never deployed military domestically. He never ignored Congress. Yet *he* was called a tyrant, while Trump's actions (if accurately described) are far worse.

**But Adams would also critique Sanders harshly**:

"Senator, you stand before millions calling for mass resistance against 'oligarchs' and 'billionaires.' Do you not see the danger? You're not calling for constitutional reform—you're calling for class warfare. You're mobilizing passion against property. **This is how republics die**."

Adams believed that pure democracy leads to mob rule, which leads to tyranny. He thought the Constitution needed to balance popular will with institutional restraint and property rights. Sanders's speech would sound to Adams like dangerous Jacobin radicalism—the French Revolution's chaos, not the American Revolution's ordered liberty.

**Adams's ambivalence**: Trump threatens institutions (bad), but Sanders mobilizes masses against the wealthy (also dangerous). Who protects the republic when both demagogues and populist movements threaten stability?

**His likely advice**: "Reform, yes. Mass movements, no. Strengthen Congress. Enforce the Constitution. But for God's sake, don't promise the people that government will solve all their economic problems through wealth redistribution. That road leads to despotism, just from a different direction."

**Adams would spend the next month writing long, anxious letters to Abigail about how the republic is doomed.**

---

## **Alexander Hamilton** - Architect of American Finance

**His Response: Fury at the Diagnosis, Rage at the Prescription**

Hamilton would be **seething** at Sanders's speech—but not for the reasons you might expect.

**On executive power**: Hamilton actually favored a strong executive. He believed presidents needed energy, decisiveness, and authority to govern effectively. But—**crucially**—he believed executive power must be constrained by law and exercised for the national interest, not personal benefit.

Sanders's description of Trump accepting a $400 million plane from Qatar while approving their Air Force facility would make Hamilton **livid**. This is corruption, plain and simple. Hamilton's entire financial system was built on the principle that **public credit requires public trust**. Personal enrichment from office destroys that trust.

Hamilton would support aggressive congressional oversight and even impeachment if Trump is using office for personal gain. "I designed the Treasury Department to be incorruptible. If the presidency itself has become a grift, burn it down and start over."

**But on Sanders's economic vision**: Hamilton would view it as catastrophic insanity.

Sanders wants to:
- Massively tax the wealthy
- Expand federal entitlements
- Guarantee healthcare, housing, education as rights
- Shrink the power of capital

**This is everything Hamilton opposed**. He built America's financial system specifically to:
- Attract capital investment
- Reward commercial enterprise  
- Create a strong industrial base
- Make America economically powerful

Hamilton would argue: "Senator Sanders, you're describing how to make America into Venezuela. Yes, wealth inequality is problematic—but your solution is to punish success, expand government dependency, and make America hostile to the very innovation and enterprise that creates prosperity. **Elon Musk didn't steal his wealth—he built companies that revolutionized electric vehicles and space travel. Jeff Bezos created a logistics network that made commerce more efficient.** Should they pay reasonable taxes? Yes. Should we confiscate their wealth to fund massive entitlements? Absolutely not."

**Hamilton's counterargument to Sanders**:

"You want to fight oligarchy? Here's how: **Break up monopolies through antitrust enforcement.** I'm a capitalist, but I believe in competition. If Bezos or Zuckerberg have monopolistic control, use government power to create competitive markets—don't nationalize or overtax to death.

**Invest in infrastructure and education** so more Americans can compete in the modern economy. Don't create dependency on government programs.

**Reform the tax code intelligently**—close loopholes, eliminate crony tax breaks, make the system more efficient. But don't wage class warfare.

**And for God's sake**, don't destroy American innovation to fund European-style social democracy. We need to be the economic leader of the world, not a declining welfare state."

**Hamilton's ultimate position**: Trump's corruption must be fought ruthlessly. But Sanders's prescription would cure the disease by killing the patient.

---

## **John Jay** - First Chief Justice, Quiet Institutionalist

**His Response: "Where Are the Courts? Where Is Congress?"**

Jay would read Sanders's speech and ask the most important question no one is answering: **"If Trump is violating the Constitution as described, why hasn't he been impeached? Why haven't courts stopped him? Why is Bernie giving a speech instead of filing lawsuits?"**

Jay spent his career building institutions that operate through law, not passion. He negotiated the Treaty of Paris and Jay's Treaty through careful diplomacy, not public rallies. He established the Supreme Court as a body that settles disputes through legal reasoning, not popular will.

**Jay's systematic response**:

**1. Document every violation**
- If Trump is ignoring congressional appropriations: lawsuit
- If he's deploying military domestically: lawsuit  
- If he's threatening judges: grounds for impeachment
- If he's accepting foreign gifts: Emoluments Clause violation, lawsuit

**2. Use constitutional mechanisms**
- Congress has appropriations power—use it
- Courts have judicial review—petition them
- States have concurrent powers—exercise them
- Impeachment exists for exactly this scenario

**3. Build the legal case systematically**
Sanders lists a dozen alleged constitutional violations. Jay would say: "Pick the three most egregious, document them thoroughly, and take Trump to court. Get rulings. Create precedent. **Make this a legal battle, not a political rally**."

**Jay's critique of Sanders**: "You're a Senator. You have institutional power. You can subpoena witnesses, hold hearings, draft articles of impeachment, file lawsuits. Instead, you're in the street giving speeches. **Use your constitutional authority or resign and become a full-time activist**."

**Jay's critique of the rallies**: "Mass protests have their place, but they're not a substitute for legal process. If millions are in the streets but Congress does nothing and courts are intimidated, you don't have a protest movement—you have pre-revolutionary conditions. That helps no one."

**Jay's prescription**: 
- Congress: Impeach or stop complaining
- States: Sue in federal court on every violation
- Courts: Issue clear rulings and dare Trump to defy them
- Public: Support elected representatives who use constitutional tools

**Jay's warning**: "The founders gave you institutions to fight tyranny. If you abandon those institutions for mass movements, you're not defending the Constitution—you're admitting it's already dead. **Prove the system still works by using it.**"

If Trump is violating the Constitution and Congress does nothing, **Congress is complicit**. The founders designed a system where Congress checks executive power. If it won't, the system fails.

### ✅ **Corruption must be fought ruthlessly**
The $400 million plane from Qatar, foreign gifts, using office for personal enrichment—**zero tolerance**. Public service requires public trust.
