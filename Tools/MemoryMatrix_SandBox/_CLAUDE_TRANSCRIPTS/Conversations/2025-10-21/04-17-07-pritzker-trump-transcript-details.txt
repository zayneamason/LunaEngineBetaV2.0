# The Founding Fathers Council Convenes on the Pritzker-Trump Crisis

## Overview of the Situation

Governor J.B. Pritzker of Illinois stands defiant against President Trump's threatened deployment of federalized National Guard troops to Chicago—allegedly to combat an "insurrection" that, by all accounts, does not exist. The governor, flanked by retired military leaders, argues this represents an unprecedented abuse of federal power, comparing Trump's tactics to authoritarian regimes. Trump claims emergency powers; Pritzker invokes the 10th Amendment and states' rights. The core question: Can a president federalize troops to deploy against American civilians in cities where local leadership opposes such action?

---

## **George Washington** - First President, Commander-in-Chief

**"This Violates Everything We Fought For"**

Washington would be deeply disturbed by what he's witnessing. When he accepted command of the Continental Army in 1775, and later the presidency, his greatest fear was always the specter of military tyranny. His farewell address warned against the "alternate domination of one faction over another" and the use of power for partisan revenge.

The key principle Washington established: **civilian control of the military, and the military's political neutrality.** He voluntarily surrendered his sword to Congress after defeating the British—setting the precedent that military power serves the people, not the executive. He refused to become king precisely to prevent what he's now seeing described: a chief executive using military force against domestic political opponents.

Washington would note that the historical precedents Pritzker cited—LBJ protecting civil rights marchers, Bush responding to the Rodney King riots—were actions taken *with* state cooperation or *at state request*, to protect constitutional rights or restore order during actual riots. Trump's threatened action is fundamentally different: deploying troops *against* state wishes, targeting peaceful protesters and political opponents, creating the very chaos he claims to be preventing.

What would alarm Washington most: the blurring of lines between federal agents and military forces, the use of masked agents acting as "secret police," and the explicit goal of punishing cities that voted against the president. This is precisely the tyrannical behavior he fought a revolution to prevent. His advice: "Governors must stand firm. The states exist as a check against federal overreach. This is why we created a federal system."

## **James Madison** - Father of the Constitution, Author of the Bill of Rights

**"The System Is Working Exactly as Designed"**

Madison would approach this with his characteristic systematic analysis. He designed the Constitution with precisely this scenario in mind: an ambitious executive attempting to consolidate power through military force. The framework intentionally makes such actions difficult.

Madison would methodically analyze the constitutional questions:

**The Insurrection Act**: The President can deploy troops domestically, but only under specific circumstances—actual insurrection, invasion, or when states cannot maintain order. Madison would examine whether any of these conditions exist in Chicago. From Pritzker's description, there is no rebellion, no invasion, and local law enforcement is functional. The "chaos" appears manufactured by federal agents themselves—using tear gas and arresting journalists—to create justification for military intervention.

**The 10th Amendment Problem**: Powers not delegated to the federal government belong to the states. Law enforcement is fundamentally a state power. Madison designed federalism precisely so states could resist federal overreach. He'd note that Pritzker's resistance isn't rebellion—it's the system functioning as intended. The governor is exercising his constitutional duty to protect his citizens from unlawful federal action.

**The Separation of Powers**: Madison would be alarmed by reports that Trump is threatening to jail governors, bypass Congress on appropriations, and intimidate judges. These aren't separate issues—they're part of a pattern of one branch attempting to dominate the others. "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition," Madison wrote. The courts blocking these actions, Congress resisting, and governors standing firm—this is the machinery of checks and balances in operation.

Madison would be particularly concerned about the precedent. His deepest fear was faction—one group using government power to oppress another. Using military force against political opponents in cities that didn't vote for you is factional tyranny made manifest. If allowed, it destroys the entire constitutional structure.

His counsel: "The Constitution provides remedies. The courts must rule. Congress must investigate. States must resist. The people must remain vigilant. The system will hold if we trust it."

## **Alexander Hamilton** - First Treasury Secretary, Military Strategist

**"This Is Incompetent Authoritarianism"**

Hamilton would have complex reactions to this situation. On one hand, he believed in strong federal power and executive authority—more so than any other Founder. He supported using federal troops to suppress the Whiskey Rebellion. On the other hand, he was brilliant, strategic, and would recognize what he's seeing as both illegal and catastrophically stupid.

Hamilton's analysis would be brutally practical:

**Military Misuse**: Hamilton understood military force. He led troops in combat and served as Washington's chief of staff. His first observation: "This isn't how you use military power." National Guard soldiers are being pulled from their communities—teachers leaving classrooms, nurses leaving hospitals—for a mission that has no military objective. There's no enemy to defeat, no territory to secure, no tactical goal. You're asking 19-year-old soldiers to make split-second decisions about constitutional law while facing American protesters. This is how you destroy military morale and public trust in the armed forces.

**Strategic Incompetence**: If Trump actually wanted to address immigration or crime in Chicago, there are dozens of legal, effective approaches. Hamilton would list them: coordinated law enforcement task forces, federal funding for local police, targeting specific criminal organizations, working *with* local authorities. Instead, Trump is using the most extreme, controversial, legally dubious method—guaranteed to create massive resistance, tie up federal resources in court battles, and unite his opponents. "This isn't governance," Hamilton would say. "This is performance art for the base, executed with no understanding of either law or strategy."

**The Authoritarian Problem**: Here's where Hamilton would surprise people. Yes, he favored strong executive power—but *within constitutional bounds* and exercised *competently*. What he's seeing is an executive who thinks strength means flouting law, when actually it means understanding and mastering law. Hamilton wrote extensive constitutional arguments for his policies. Trump is just declaring emergency and expecting compliance. That's not strength—it's weakness pretending to be strength.

Hamilton would also note the corruption: "$400 million plane from Qatar? Building their Air Force base in Idaho?" His response: "I built an entire financial system on the principle that America would honor its obligations and maintain credibility. This man is for sale, and everyone knows it. That's not just morally corrupt—it's strategically suicidal. Who will trust America's word now?"

His final assessment: "If you're going to seize power, at least be competent about it. This is just chaos serving ego. The republic will survive him, but the damage to our institutions will take decades to repair."

## **Thomas Jefferson** - Author of the Declaration, Champion of States' Rights

**"The Spirit of 1776 Lives in Illinois"**

Jefferson would be absolutely fierce in his support of Governor Pritzker. This scenario triggers every one of his deepest political convictions: federal overreach, military tyranny, suppression of dissent, punishment of political opponents, and violation of states' rights.

Jefferson's first observation would be historical: "In 1776, we declared independence because King George sent troops to enforce his will on the colonies against our consent. The Declaration of Independence lists this as a grievance: 'He has kept among us, in times of peace, Standing Armies without the Consent of our legislatures.' Now a President is doing exactly the same—sending troops into a state against the wishes of its elected government to intimidate civilians. We fought a revolution to stop this."

Jefferson would embrace Pritzker's analogy to authoritarian regimes without hesitation. He'd point to his own observations of tyranny in Europe and argue that authoritarian consolidation always follows this pattern: create the perception of chaos, deploy security forces against "enemies," intimidate press and judges, undermine elections, concentrate power. "Trump is following the playbook," Jefferson would say. "The only question is whether Americans will recognize it in time."

**On Federal Agents as Secret Police**: Jefferson would be horrified by the description of masked ICE and CBP agents in military-style uniforms, stopping people based on skin color, arresting journalists, using tear gas on communities. "These are the tactics of tyranny," he'd argue. "When agents of the government operate in masks, without accountability, without due process, terrorizing citizens—this is how free societies die. The press must expose them. The courts must stop them. The people must resist them."

**On States' Rights**: This is where Jefferson would plant his flag. "The states exist precisely to check federal power. When the federal government becomes tyrannical, the states are the last line of defense for liberty. Governor Pritzker is not rebelling—he is doing his constitutional duty. Illinois is not his to conquer because Illinois belongs to its citizens, not to any president."

Jefferson would call for massive resistance: "Every state governor should stand with Illinois. Every newspaper should expose these abuses. Every citizen should protest peacefully but firmly. This is the moment that tests whether we're still a free people or whether we'll submit to tyranny wrapped in the flag."

## **John Jay** - First Chief Justice, Diplomat, Federalist

**"The Courts Must Act Decisively"**

Jay would approach this with his characteristic focus on legal order and institutional stability. As the first Chief Justice, he helped establish the principle that courts interpret the Constitution and check executive power. He'd see this crisis as exactly the moment the judiciary was designed for.

Jay's analysis would focus on legal mechanisms:

**Immediate Injunctions**: He'd note that a federal judge has already issued a temporary restraining order requiring body cameras on federal agents. "This is the judiciary doing its job—imposing accountability and transparency on executive action." Jay would call for more aggressive judicial intervention: injunctions against federalizing troops without clear statutory authority, orders protecting journalists and protesters, contempt citations for officials who violate constitutional rights.

**Habeas Corpus**: The reports of people being "grabbed, arrested, taken to god knows where" would trigger Jay's concern about fundamental legal protections. "Every person arrested must be brought before a judge. Every detention must have legal basis. Every family must know where their loved one is being held. If federal agents are operating beyond these rules, judges must intervene immediately."

**Separation of Powers**: Jay would analyze Trump's broader pattern—threatening judges, ignoring congressional appropriations, punishing states for political reasons. "Each of these is potentially an impeachable offense. Together, they constitute a systematic assault on constitutional governance. Congress has a duty to investigate and, if warranted, impeach. The Senate must hold him accountable."

Jay's strategic advice would be practical: "Governor Pritzker should pursue every legal avenue. File in state court, federal court, appeal immediately. The Supreme Court will ultimately decide whether a president can deploy military forces against domestic political opponents without genuine insurrection. Make them rule."

But Jay would also be realistic about institutional limits: "The Constitution provides checks, but they require courage to use. If Congress won't act, if courts are intimidated, if governors submit, then the parchment means nothing. Institutions only work when people defend them."

## **Benjamin Franklin** - Diplomat, Wit, Elder Statesman

**"This Foolishness Will Destroy Him"**

Franklin, at his wisest in old age, would observe this situation with a mix of concern and wry insight. He lived through actual tyranny, helped craft the Constitution, and understood human nature deeply.

His first reaction would be characteristically blunt: "Trump is a fool. You cannot govern a free people through intimidation. You cannot unite a nation by deploying troops against citizens. You cannot build lasting power by making enemies of half the country. This approach inevitably destroys itself—the only question is how much damage it does first."

Franklin would note the self-defeating nature of Trump's tactics: "He claims he needs military force because of chaos—but federal agents are creating the chaos. He says he's protecting Americans—but Americans are protesting *against* his agents. He wants to look strong—but he's advertising his weakness. A truly powerful leader doesn't need masked agents and military troops to control a city. He's showing everyone he's lost the consent of the governed."

**On Trump's Character**: Franklin had extensive experience with vain, ambitious men seeking glory. "Trump reminds me of certain European princes—all grandeur and no substance, all gesture and no strategy. He craves the symbols of power while understanding nothing of its actual exercise. Real leaders don't need to threaten judges and jail governors—their authority comes from the trust of the people they serve."

**On the Military Leaders**: Franklin would appreciate the retired generals standing with Pritzker. "These men understand something Trump never will: the military's power comes from its integrity. Once soldiers are seen as political weapons, once they're used against fellow citizens, the trust that makes them effective is destroyed. These generals are trying to save the military from Trump's corruption of it."

Franklin's advice would mix practical wisdom with moral clarity: "To the American people: Do not be afraid. Tyrants rule through fear, but fear only works if you submit to it. Peacefully resist. Support your local leaders. Trust your institutions while demanding they act courageously. This storm will pass—but only if you weather it with dignity and determination."

"To Trump: You are destroying the very power you seek. Every threat alienates more Americans. Every abuse creates more resistance. Every lie reduces your credibility. You are building a legacy of failure wrapped in cruelty. History will not be kind."

## Areas of Agreement Among the Founders

Despite their many differences, the Founders would find remarkable consensus:

1. **This Is Not a Legitimate Emergency**: There is no insurrection in Chicago. Federal military deployment requires actual rebellion, invasion, or state inability to maintain order. None exist here.

2. **States' Rights Must Be Respected**: The federal system exists precisely so states can resist federal overreach. Governor Pritzker is exercising his constitutional role, not rebelling against it.

3. **Military Must Remain Above Politics**: Using troops to punish political opponents and cities that voted against you destroys the military's integrity and threatens constitutional governance.

4. **The Pattern Matters**: Threatening governors, intimidating judges, bypassing Congress, suppressing press—these aren't isolated incidents but a systematic attempt to concentrate power beyond constitutional limits.

5. **The People Must Resist**: Peaceful protest, legal challenges, political organizing, and civic courage are precisely how free people defend freedom. The protesters and local leaders standing firm are acting in the best traditions of American liberty.

## The Key Disagreement

The Founders would debate *how far* resistance should go:

**Jefferson** would support aggressive state resistance, including potentially refusing to comply with federal orders he deems unconstitutional. "The states can nullify unconstitutional acts."

**Madison** would urge working through legal and political channels. "The system provides remedies. Use them exhaustively before considering extralegal resistance."

**Hamilton** would want decisive federal court intervention and, if necessary, congressional impeachment. "Clear legal authority must prevail, even if it means removing a president."

**Washington** would call for national unity against tyranny but would be cautious about any steps that might trigger actual violence. "We must defend the Constitution, but we must also preserve the nation."

## The Collective Wisdom

These Founders fought a revolution to establish a system where power belongs to the people, exercised through elected representatives, constrained by law, and divided among competing institutions precisely so no single person could rule as a king. They are watching a President attempt to do exactly what they designed the Constitution to prevent.

Their message to Americans in 2025: **The test of a constitutional republic comes when an ambitious executive tests its limits. The Constitution provides the tools—courts, Congress, states, elections, and the First Amendment. But parchment alone cannot defend itself. It requires citizens and officials with the courage to use those tools, whatever the personal cost.**

As Madison would say: "This is the moment the system was designed for. We gave you a republic—if you can keep it."
