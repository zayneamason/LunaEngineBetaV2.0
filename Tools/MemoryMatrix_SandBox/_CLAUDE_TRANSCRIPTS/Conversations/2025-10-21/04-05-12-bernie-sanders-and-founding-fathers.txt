 # The Founding Fathers Council on Bernie Sanders' "No Kings" Protest Speech

## Context

Senator Bernie Sanders delivered this speech at a massive protest in Washington DC in 2025, responding to concerns about executive overreach, consolidation of power, and the influence of billionaires in American politics. The speech invoked the founders' own rejection of monarchy and warned of authoritarianism.

---

## George Washington - First President, Commander-in-Chief

I find myself both stirred and troubled by Senator Sanders' invocation of our struggle against King George.

**On the core message**: He is right to sound the alarm about concentrated power. I spent eight years resisting those who wished me to become a king or president-for-life. When officers at Newburgh wanted me to seize power, I refused—that moment defined what America would be. I told them explicitly that "the reign" of any strongman must be "over," and I chose to walk away from power when I could have grasped more. Senator Sanders correctly identifies this as our founding principle.

**My concern about his methods**: While I valued loyalty and showed up when duty called, I avoided confrontation when possible and took criticism personally. Mass protests have their place, but I would caution against rhetoric that further divides. When I faced the Whiskey Rebellion, I used force only after exhausting peaceful means. The senator's inflammatory language about "divine right of oligarchs" and calling for resistance may inflame rather than unite.

**On wealth and oligarchy**: This troubles me most. I was myself a wealthy Virginia planter who managed enslaved people—a moral weight I increasingly struggled with in my later years. I understand his concern about concentrated wealth influencing government. Yet I also built this nation alongside men of property because we believed ordered liberty required stakeholders in society's success. The question is not whether wealth should exist, but whether it corrupts the public good.

**My verdict**: The senator's warning about unchecked executive power echoes my own deepest fears. But his solution—mass mobilization and class warfare—risks the very chaos that destroys republics. I would rather have been on my farm than emperor of the world. Power must be constrained by character, not just by crowds.

---

## Thomas Jefferson - Author of the Declaration of Independence

Senator Sanders speaks my language—perhaps too well for comfort.

**On resistance to tyranny**: I wrote that when government becomes destructive of rights, the people have the right to alter or abolish it. I believed a little rebellion now and then is a good thing—the tree of liberty must be refreshed from time to time. Sanders channels this spirit beautifully. His list of executive overreach—threatening judges, ignoring Congress, attacking the press—these are precisely the abuses I detailed against King George in the Declaration.

**On the oligarchy of wealth**: Here Sanders strikes at the heart of my deepest concerns. I feared what Hamilton was building—a financial system that would create an aristocracy of wealth, where paper fortunes and speculation would replace honest agriculture and widespread property ownership. The concentration of wealth Sanders describes—one man owning more than 52% of households—this is the nightmare I warned against. When Hamilton and I fought over the national bank and federal power, I was fighting to prevent exactly this: a system where financial elites control government.

**My profound contradiction**: Yet I must acknowledge what Sanders does not mention—I enslaved over 600 people and benefited from that injustice until my death. I wrote eloquently about liberty while denying it to those in my own household. When Sanders speaks of 15 million losing healthcare, of childhood poverty, of workers struggling—I hear echoes of my own hypocrisy. I spoke beautiful words about equality but lacked the courage to pay their price.

**My assessment**: Sanders is more consistent than I was. He connects his principles to action in ways I failed to do. But I would caution him: I intellectualized everything, even love, into philosophical treatises. Sometimes the head must give way to the heart. His diagnosis is sound, but his remedy—federal power to guarantee healthcare, housing, education—this is precisely the centralization I spent my life opposing. The question remains: how do we protect liberty from both government tyranny AND private oligarchy?

---

## Benjamin Franklin - Diplomat, Inventor, Sage

Well, this is delightful! Finally, someone's talking sense about these pompous asses with too much money.

**On practical wisdom**: I never patented my inventions because I believed knowledge should serve the public good. "As we enjoy great advantages from the inventions of others, we should be glad of an opportunity to serve others by any invention of ours; and this we should do freely and generously." Sanders grasps this principle. When he speaks of billionaires hoarding wealth while children go hungry, he's echoing my view that private gain must not override public benefit.

**On oligarchy**: I would be that guy who posts "I made a thing" and open-sources everything on GitHub. I'd have a Substack about practical innovation. Modern billionaires are doing the opposite—they're enclosing the commons, privatizing knowledge, extracting wealth rather than creating shared value. Musk's fortune rests on government subsidies and infrastructure he didn't build. Bezos exploits workers in warehouses. These aren't the self-made men I respect—they're rent-seekers.
