Now I have information about Mach3 CNC software. Here's what it is:

Mach3 is popular CNC control software that turns a standard Windows PC into a fully-featured CNC machine controller. It works on most Windows PCs to control the motion of motors (stepper & servo) by processing G-Code. 

Key features and capabilities include:

- Converts a standard PC into a fully featured, 6-axis CNC controller
- Allows direct import of DXF, BMP, JPG, and HPGL files through LazyCam
- Customizable interface that can be used for many applications with various types of hardware
- Support for "Wizards" which are mini-programs that extend capabilities and allow users to perform operations without pre-written G-Code

The software is available in a demo version with limitations:
- Mill & Plasma: 500 line G-code limit (10,000,000 after licensing)
- Turn: 50 line G-code limit (10,000,000 after licensing)
- Kernel Frequency locked at 25kHz (only applicable if using the parallel port driver)

Licensing information:
Non-commercial users (hobbyists) can use one Mach license for multiple machines, while commercial users need a separate license for each machine.

System requirements:
- Desktop PC with at least one parallel port (or a compatible motion controller board)
- 32-bit version of Windows (Windows 2000, XP, Vista, 7)
The software is also compatible with Windows 8 and Windows 10

It's worth noting that Mach4 is also available, which is completely new software with less than 1% of the programming code in common with Mach3. It was written from the ground up to be expandable, flexible, and extremely responsive for use with very large files.

This indicates a USB communication problem between your computer and the SmoothStepper device. Common causes for this error include:

1. Electrical noise issues - This is one of the most common causes. USB connections can be susceptible to electrical noise from your CNC machine, especially during operation.

2. Power supply problems - Poor or noisy voltage to either the SmoothStepper or your breakout board can cause USB connections to drop.

3. USB cable issues - The cable could be damaged, too long, or of poor quality. Avoid using retractable/wind-up USB cables as they can cause noise and data errors.

4. USB connection problems - Try connecting directly to your PC instead of through a USB hub, and try different USB ports on your computer.

5. Grounding problems - According to user discussions, grounding issues are a frequent cause of these errors with USB SmoothSteppers.

6. Driver issues - You might need to uninstall and reinstall the SmoothStepper USB drivers.

Potential solutions:

1. Check all ground connections in your system
2. Try a different, high-quality USB cable
3. Connect to a different USB port directly on your computer (not through a hub)
4. Ensure your power supplies are clean and stable
5. Reinstall the SmoothStepper drivers
6. Consider upgrading to an Ethernet SmoothStepper (ESS) instead of USB, as the Ethernet version doesn't have direct electrical connections via the cable (it's transformer-coupled) and is generally considered more reliable in noisy CNC environments.

Based on my research, I can provide information about the default Ports and Pins configuration in Mach3 when using a SmoothStepper USB (USS) controller.

The SmoothStepper USB is designed to be a direct replacement for parallel ports in Mach3, so one of its key advantages is that if you're moving from a parallel port setup to SmoothStepper, "the ports & pins setup under Mach3 for the printer port should work without change with the SmoothStepper."

Here are the important details about the default configuration:

1. Port Structure:
   - The USB SmoothStepper has 2 parallel ports and a third dedicated input port
   - Ports 1 and 2 appear like standard parallel ports to Mach3
   - Port 3 is a dedicated input port on the USB SmoothStepper

2. Default Pin Configuration:
   - Dedicated Output Pins: Pins 1, 14, 16, and 17 of ports 1 and 2, plus pins 2 through 9 of port 1
   - Dedicated Input Pins: Pins 10, 11, 12, 13, and 15 of ports 1 and 2
   - Bidirectional Pins: Pins 2 through 9 on Port 2 are bidirectional pins that default to inputs

3. Important configuration notes:
   - You can choose to change the bidirectional pins as a group to be all inputs or all outputs through Mach3's configuration
   - When the USS is powered but not controlled by Mach3, the dedicated output pins are pulled low (to GND) and dedicated input pins are pulled high (to about 4.5-5.5V)

For the error you're experiencing with the FT_IO_ERROR in the USB communication, the default port and pin settings aren't likely the direct cause of the problem. The issue is more related to the USB communication between your computer and the SmoothStepper device, rather than the signal pin configuration. Based on my research, here are the specifics for configuring the E-stop pin with your SmoothStepper USB in Mach3:

### E-stop Configuration Steps:

1. Go to Menu -> Config -> Ports and Pins -> Inputs in Mach3

2. For the "Emergency" input signal, you need to assign a port and pin number. Any of pins 10, 11, 12, 13, and 15 of Port 1 or Port 2 will work as these are dedicated input pins on the SmoothStepper USB.

3. Set the "Port" field to your desired SmoothStepper USB port (1 or 2)

4. Set the "Pin" field to your desired input pin (10, 11, 12, 13, or 15 are recommended for inputs)

5. Set "Active Low" depending on your hardware. This determines whether your E-stop is triggered when the signal goes low (typical for normally closed switches) or high.

6. After configuration, exit Mach3 to save your settings, then reopen it

7. Testing: Press the Red Reset button in the lower left corner of Mach3. The flashing red border should change to a solid green border (indicating the SmoothStepper is communicating with Mach3). When you press your hardware E-stop button, the Mach3 Reset button should start flashing red again.

8. You can also confirm the E-stop is working by checking the Mach3 Diagnostic tab to see if the Emergency signal turns on and off when you activate the E-stop button.

### Important Safety Notes:

The SmoothStepper developers emphasize that software alone is not a reliable way to stop motors in an emergency. Your E-stop button should physically disconnect power to your motors and other output devices (spindles, plasma cutters, lasers, etc.) through hardware.

The E-stop input on the SmoothStepper is primarily a "courtesy signal" to inform the software that an emergency stop has occurred, but should not be relied upon as the only safety mechanism.
