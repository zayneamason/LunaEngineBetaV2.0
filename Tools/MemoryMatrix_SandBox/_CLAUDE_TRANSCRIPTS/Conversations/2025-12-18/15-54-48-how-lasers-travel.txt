how do lasers travel?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[ASSISTANT] 2025-12-18T15:55:10.096573Z
 Lasers travel as coherent electromagnetic radiation—photons moving in phase with each other in a tight, parallel beam.

The key properties:

**Coherence** — All the light waves are synchronized in phase and frequency, unlike regular light where waves are jumbled. This is why laser beams stay tight over distance instead of spreading like a flashlight.

**Collimation** — The beam is highly parallel. Photons are emitted in essentially the same direction, so divergence is minimal (though not zero—even lasers spread slightly over long distances).

**Speed** — Same as all light: ~299,792 km/s in vacuum. Slows slightly in denser media (glass, water, air).

**Medium interaction** — In atmosphere, lasers scatter off particles (dust, moisture), which is why you can "see" a beam in fog or smoke. In vacuum, the beam is invisible unless it hits something.

For your fabrication context: if you're working with cutting/engraving lasers, the beam gets focused through optics to concentrate energy at a focal point—that's where the magic happens for material processing. The coherence is what lets you focus it so precisely.

one direction or back and forth like electricity?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[ASSISTANT] 2025-12-18T15:55:48.622392Z
 One direction—straight line propagation.

Photons shoot out and keep going until they hit something (absorbed, reflected, or scattered). No oscillation back and forth along the travel path like AC current.

The "wave" part that might be confusing: the electromagnetic field oscillates *perpendicular* to the direction of travel (that's what makes it a wave), but the energy/photons themselves are moving forward continuously. Think of it like a snake slithering forward—the body wiggles side to side, but the snake is definitely heading *that way*.

Electricity in a wire is different—electrons actually drift back and forth in AC, and even in DC they're bouncing around chaotically while net-drifting one direction. Photons don't do that. They're born, they go, they die when they hit something.

what happens when they bounce?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[ASSISTANT] 2025-12-18T15:56:52.603050Z
 Angle of incidence equals angle of reflection—billiard ball physics, basically.

**Specular reflection** (mirrors, polished metal): Photons bounce off in a predictable direction. The beam stays coherent, which is why you can redirect lasers with mirrors in your cutting setups.

**Diffuse reflection** (rough surfaces): Photons scatter in all directions because the surface is microscopically uneven. Each photon still follows the angle rule, but the surface angles are random. This is why you can see the laser dot on a wall—light is bouncing toward your eyes from all angles.

**What actually happens at the atomic level**: The photon gets absorbed by electrons in the material, which jump to a higher energy state, then immediately drop back down and emit a new photon. It's not technically the *same* photon bouncing—it's absorption and re-emission happening so fast it looks like a bounce.

For highly reflective materials (why you don't cut copper or aluminum easily with CO2 lasers): too much energy bounces back instead of being absorbed, so the material doesn't heat up efficiently. Fiber lasers work better on metals partly because of wavelength—the material absorbs that frequency more readily.

[HUMAN] 2025-12-18T15:59:54.022764Z
wait every time it bounces it changes?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[ASSISTANT] 2025-12-18T16:00:02.999546Z
 Yeah, technically it's a different photon.

The incoming photon gets absorbed—it's gone, its energy is transferred to an electron in the material. That electron jumps up an energy level, then immediately drops back down and releases a new photon.

For a good mirror, this happens so fast and so consistently that:
- Same wavelength (color)
- Same direction (angle preserved)
- Coherence mostly maintained

So *functionally* it's the same beam, but philosophically? New photons every bounce.

It's kind of like saying "is this the same wave after it bounces off the pool wall?" The water molecules aren't traveling across the pool—energy is being transferred through the medium and re-expressed.

This is also why even "perfect" mirrors absorb a tiny bit with each reflection—not every photon gets re-emitted. Some energy becomes heat. Stack enough bounces and you lose the beam.

Here's a fun one: this is how lasers *work* in the first place. Inside the laser cavity, photons are bouncing between mirrors, stimulating more atoms to emit *more* photons in sync. The "bouncing" is really a chain reaction of absorb-emit-absorb-emit, amplifying until you get that coherent beam blasting out.

**Laser cutting/engraving (probably most relevant to you)**
- Pathfinding and toolpath optimization (traveling salesman-style problems—minimizing head movement)
- Kerf compensation algorithms
- Power/speed ramping for corners and curves
- Dithering algorithms for grayscale engraving (converting images to on/off pulse patterns)

**Lidar/scanning**
- SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) for autonomous vehicles and robots
- Point cloud processing and surface reconstruction
- Time-of-flight calculations

**Laser communication**
- Error correction codes
- Modulation/demodulation for encoding data on the beam
- Adaptive optics algorithms to compensate for atmospheric distortion

**Industrial/scientific**
- Interferometry analysis (measuring nanometer-scale changes)
- Spectroscopy pattern matching
- Laser control loops (PID controllers maintaining stable output)
