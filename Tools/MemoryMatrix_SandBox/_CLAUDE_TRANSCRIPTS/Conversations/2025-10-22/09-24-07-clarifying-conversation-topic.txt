# **THE FOUNDING FATHERS COUNCIL**
## Analysis of the Senate Debate: Term Length and Stability

### **Opening Context**

This is the Constitutional Convention's debate on **how long senators should serve** - a seemingly technical question that cut to the heart of what kind of republic America would become. The stakes? Whether the new government would protect liberty through democracy or descend into mob rule; whether property rights would be safeguarded or confiscated; whether wisdom would check passion or passion would consume everything.

---

## **JAMES MADISON** - *Architect of the Constitution, Age 36*

**His Position:**

I argued for long senatorial terms - as long as possible, ideally for life during good behavior. This transcript captures my core conviction: **we are designing for ages, not for the moment**.

Look at my argument carefully: "The government we mean to erect is intended to last for ages." This wasn't rhetorical flourish - this was engineering. I had studied every failed republic in history. I knew exactly where democratic experiments collapsed: when temporary majorities destroyed permanent interests.

**My Warning:**

The "landed interest" currently dominates, but "in process of time, when we approximate to the states and kingdoms of Europe... when the number of landholders shall be comparatively small through the various means of trade and manufactures" - what then? Without a stable Senate to protect minority rights, you get pure democracy. And pure democracy is two wolves and a lamb voting on dinner.

**The Uncomfortable Truth:**

I was explicitly arguing that the Senate should "protect the minority of the opulent against the majority." I won't pretend otherwise. I believed - *still believe* - that property rights are foundational to all other rights. An "agrarian law" (wealth redistribution) would destroy the incentive to industry and ultimately collapse the system.

**But notice what I'm actually proposing:** Not a hereditary aristocracy. Not titles. Not permanent class divisions. Simply a Senate with enough stability to resist momentary passions and demagogues. As long as offices remain open to all through merit, it remains republican.

**My Method Here:**

See how I work? Start with first principles ("republican form"), acknowledge objections ("there is weight in the gentleman from South Carolina"), provide historical evidence (I'd studied every confederation), appeal to long-term thinking, and construct a systematic solution. This is how you build institutions that last centuries.

---

## **ALEXANDER HAMILTON** - *Former Artillery Captain, Age 30*

**My Position:**

Let me be blunt, as usual: **this convention is not radical enough**.

When I said "if we incline too much to democracy, we shall soon shoot into a monarchy," I meant it literally. History shows this pattern repeatedly: excessive democracy creates chaos, chaos demands order, and suddenly you have a tyrant.

**My Actual Preference:**

I wanted senators for life. I wanted the President for life. I wanted something approaching the British system - which is, despite what Tom Jefferson says from his safe perch in Paris, the best government ever devised.

"Real liberty is neither found in despotism or the extremes of democracy, but in moderate governments." That's not theory - that's reality.

**The Economic Reality:**

"The difference of property is already great amongst us. Commerce and industry will still increase the disparity." This isn't pessimism; it's mathematics. Capital compounds. Wealth concentrates. Your government must be designed for this reality, not for some agrarian fantasy Jefferson writes poetry about.

**My Critique of Sherman:**

Connecticut's system worked because Connecticut was tiny and homogeneous and barely had to do anything! "Dare they collect the taxes and requisitions of congress?" No! "Such a government may do well, if they do not tax" - exactly! But we need a real government that can act on a continental scale.

**Why I'm Right:**

The tribunes of Rome started as protectors of the plebeians. Noble goal. What happened? Eventually Rome divided into rich and poor, and the republic collapsed. If you don't structure your government to account for economic inequality, that inequality will destroy your government.

**What I Actually Got:**

A compromise. Six-year terms, rotation. Better than nothing. Not as good as it should be. Story of my life at this convention.

---

## **GEORGE WASHINGTON** - *Convention President, Age 55*

**My Role:**

I presided over this debate but did not speak. That was intentional.

**My Observation:**

Both Madison and Hamilton articulate real dangers. I've seen democratic assemblies in action - during the war, I watched state legislatures refuse supplies, recall generals mid-campaign, and nearly lose us our independence through excessive democracy. Some stability is essential.

But I also worry when I hear talk of protecting "the minority of the opulent." I'm wealthy, yes. But I became so through land and service, not speculation. There's something unsettling in designing a government explicitly to protect the rich from the poor.

**My Instinct:**

The Senate should be stable enough to check rash action, but not so permanent that it becomes a separate aristocratic class. Six years with rotation seems reasonable. Long enough to develop wisdom, short enough to maintain accountability.

**What I Notice:**

Hamilton speaks of approaching monarchy with surprising comfort. Madison constructs elaborate defenses of property. Sherman trusts in virtue and annual elections. They're all partially right - and the final structure should incorporate elements of each.

The key is balance. Always balance.

**My Reaction (from Paris):**

I'm not there, but when I later read Madison's notes, this debate troubles me deeply.

**My Concerns:**

"Protect the minority of the opulent against the majority"? This is oligarchy dressed in republican clothing! Madison, my dear friend, has let his fear of democracy overpower his faith in the people.

**The Agrarian Vision:**

Hamilton mocks Connecticut and small-scale democracy. But that's precisely what I want to preserve! Small farmers, independent and virtuous, are the foundation of republican government. Once you accept that "commerce and industry will increase disparity," you've accepted the corruption of the republic.

**On Senate Terms:**

Yes, we need some stability. But nine years? Life tenure? These men are terrified of democracy. They're creating precisely what we fought against - a government where power flows from property, not from consent.

**The Dangerous Assumption:**

This entire debate assumes economic inequality is natural and permanent. But what if we designed a society where it *doesn't* increase? Where land is distributed, where speculation is limited, where every farmer owns his plot?

**My Alternative:**

Shorter terms. Rotation. Trust in the people. And for God's sake, stop talking about protecting the wealthy! That's not what we declared independence for!

## **JOHN ADAMS** - *Minister to Britain, Age 51 (not present)*

**My Reaction (from London):**

Reading this later, I see both wisdom and foolishness.

**Where Madison Is Right:**

Absolutely correct that factions will destroy pure democracy. I've seen it in Massachusetts - Shays' Rebellion nearly toppled the state. Some institutional check on majoritarian impulses is essential.

**Where Hamilton Goes Too Far:**

"Incline too much to democracy and you'll shoot into monarchy"? Alexander, your pessimism about human nature blinds you. Yes, we need mixed government - but you seem to want monarchy with republican window dressing!

**My Own View:**

I argued in "Defence of the Constitutions" for a strong executive and a senate representing property and wisdom. But even I wouldn't go as far as Hamilton. There's preserving order and then there's creating aristocracy.

**The Core Issue:**

They're all dancing around the real question: Can a republic survive in a large, commercial, economically unequal nation? History says no. Madison and Hamilton are trying to make it say yes through structural engineering.

I'm skeptical. But I hope they succeed.

## **BENJAMIN FRANKLIN** - *Pennsylvania Delegate, Age 81*

**My Perspective:**

I sat through this debate, and I must tell you - all this concern about "the mob" confiscating property makes me chuckle.

**The Real Danger:**

It's not the poor stealing from the rich. It's the rich stealing from the poor through political power! I've watched it happen in every government. The wealthy use state power to protect monopolies, grant exclusive privileges, and extract from labor.

**On Senate Terms:**

Make them accountable. Rotation is good. Connecticut's model has merit - Sherman is right that virtue can be rewarded without permanent power.

**What I'd Say (If I Weren't So Old):**

"Gentlemen, you're building elaborate safeguards against tyranny of the majority. Who will safeguard against tyranny of the minority? For that's what you're creating - a Senate explicitly designed to protect wealth against democracy."

But I'm tired. The young men will design their system. I'll sign it and hope for the best.

**My Hope:**

That the virtue of the American people overcomes whatever structural flaws this system contains. Good people can make a mediocre system work. Bad people will corrupt even the best design.

## **COUNCIL SYNTHESIS: Areas of Consensus and Conflict**

### **Where They Agree:**

1. **Pure democracy is dangerous** - even Jefferson (grudgingly) admits some institutional checks needed
2. **Some stability is essential** - can't have government by daily referendum
3. **Factions will emerge** - economic interests will drive political conflict
4. **The system must last** - designing for future generations, not just 1787

### **Where They Fiercely Disagree:**

**On Democracy:**
- **Hamilton/Madison:** Deep skepticism, need strong checks
- **Jefferson/Franklin:** Faith in people, minimal restraints
- **Washington/Adams:** Cautious middle ground

**On Property:**
- **Hamilton/Madison:** Must be explicitly protected from majority
- **Jefferson:** Protecting wealthy minority corrupts republicanism
- **Franklin:** Wealthy are the threat, not the poor

**On Senate Structure:**
- **Hamilton:** Life tenure preferred
- **Madison:** Very long terms (9+ years)
- **Adams:** Moderate terms with property qualification
- **Jefferson:** Short terms, rotation, accountability
- **Franklin:** Whatever keeps them honest

### **The Collective Wisdom:**

**What this debate reveals:** The Founders understood they were making trade-offs between stability and democracy, property rights and popular sovereignty, wisdom and accountability. There was no perfect answer.

**What they created:** Six-year terms with rotation - a compromise between Hamilton's aristocratic vision and Jefferson's democratic faith. Long enough for stability, short enough for accountability.

**What they feared:** That future generations would forget these trade-offs and push the system toward either extreme - pure democracy or pure oligarchy.

**What they'd tell 2025 America:**

We built this tension into the system deliberately. The Senate was *supposed* to slow things down, check popular passion, protect long-term thinking. But it was *also* supposed to remain accountable to the people, just on a longer timeline.

If your Senate has become purely obstructionist, you've lost the stability-accountability balance. If it simply reflects immediate popular will, you've lost its purpose entirely.

The question isn't whether the Senate should be democratic or stable - it must be both. The question is whether you still understand the trade-offs we were making, and whether our specific calibration (six years, staggered terms) still achieves that balance in your very different era.

**Final Note from Madison:**

I took these notes carefully because I knew future generations would need to understand *why* we made these choices. Not to worship them as sacred. But to understand the problems we were solving - so you can judge whether those same solutions still apply to your problems.

That's why I spent twenty years editing these notes in retirement. Not for glory. For clarity.

You're welcome.
