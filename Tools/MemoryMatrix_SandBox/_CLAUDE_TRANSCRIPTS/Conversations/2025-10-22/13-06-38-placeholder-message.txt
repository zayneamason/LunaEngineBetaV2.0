# The Founding Fathers Council on January 6, 2021

## Context

On January 6, 2021, as Congress convened to certify Joe Biden's electoral victory, thousands of Donald Trump supporters—incited by the President's speech claiming a stolen election—stormed the United States Capitol. The violent breach interrupted constitutional proceedings, resulted in multiple deaths, extensive property damage, and represented the first time since 1814 that the Capitol had been breached. President Trump delayed calling for peace, initially tweeting criticism of Vice President Pence for refusing to unconstitutionally overturn the election, while rioters chanted threats and ransacked congressional offices.

## **George Washington** - *First President, Commander-in-Chief*

I am filled with profound sorrow and alarm.

This assault on the Capitol strikes at the very experiment in self-government I helped establish. When we founded this Republic, we rejected the rule of kings and mobs alike. What occurred on January 6th was mob rule—the very antithesis of ordered liberty.

Throughout my presidency, I witnessed faction and party spirit threaten our Union. In my Farewell Address, I warned explicitly: "The alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by the spirit of revenge, natural to party dissension... is itself a frightful despotism." What greater despotism than a mob attempting to overturn the lawful determination of an election?

I resigned my commission as Commander-in-Chief in 1783 when I could have seized power. I voluntarily left the presidency in 1797 when I could have remained. I did so because the peaceful transfer of power—the willing surrender of authority to the people's chosen representatives—is the cornerstone of republican government. President Trump's refusal to accept electoral defeat and his incitement of violence to prevent the constitutional process represents a betrayal of everything we fought to establish.

Vice President Pence deserves commendation. When pressured to exceed his constitutional authority, he honored his oath. This is precisely the principled resistance to tyranny—even from one's own faction—that our system requires.

The President's hours of silence while violence

 unfolded, his attacks on those defending constitutional order, and his refusal to clearly condemn the mob represent a failure of the most fundamental presidential duty: to preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution. No man is above the law. No grievance, however sincerely felt, justifies violence against the constitutional order.

**This was not patriotism. It was its opposite.**

## **Thomas Jefferson** - *Author of the Declaration of Independence, Third President*

I find myself torn between competing principles.

I have written that "a little rebellion now and then is a good thing" and that "the tree of liberty must be refreshed from time to time with the blood of patriots and tyrants." Some may invoke these words to justify January 6th. They would be profoundly mistaken.

The right of revolution exists—but only against genuine tyranny when all peaceful remedies are exhausted. Was this such a moment? Emphatically no.

Consider the facts: 
- Sixty court cases examined election fraud claims—most before judges appointed by Republicans, some by Trump himself—and found them without merit
- State officials, including Republicans, certified results
- The Electoral College voted according to state laws
- Congress was exercising its constitutional duty to count electoral votes

What tyranny existed here? President Trump had access to every legal remedy. He lost not because the system was rigged, but because more citizens voted for his opponent. To call this tyranny is to declare that losing an election constitutes oppression.

The rioters claimed to act as patriots defending democracy. Yet they sought to overturn the democratic process itself. This is the paradox of their position: they would destroy the system that guarantees their liberty in order to install their preferred leader. This is not revolution against tyranny—it is tyranny disguised as revolution.

I have also championed freedom of speech and believed that government should withstand criticism. But there exists a profound difference between speech and violence, between protest and insurrection. When protesters break windows, assault officers, and hunt lawmakers through the Capitol's halls, they forfeit any claim to peaceful dissent.

President Trump's conduct disturbs me especially. A President's power over the people is immense. To wield that power to spread falsehoods about election theft, to summon a mob to the Capitol, to direct them to "fight like hell," and then to delay calling them off while celebrating their "love" and "special" nature—this represents dangerous demagoguery.

**I believed in the people's capacity for self-government. January 6th tested that faith, but also vindicated it—for in the end, constitutional order prevailed.**

## **Alexander Hamilton** - *First Treasury Secretary, Co-Author of Federalist Papers*

This is precisely the danger I warned against.

In Federalist #1, I wrote: "of those men who have overturned the liberties of republics, the greatest number have begun their career by paying an obsequious court to the people; commencing demagogues, and ending tyrants."

President Trump fits this pattern exactly. He claimed to speak for "the people" against "elites," positioned himself as their champion against a corrupt system, and when the people—through their constitutional representatives—chose differently, he incited violence to overturn their choice.

Let me be unsparing: **This was an attempted coup.**

Not a military coup, but a constitutional coup. The goal was to prevent the lawful certification of electoral votes through intimidation, violence, and chaos. That it failed does not diminish its intent.

I have little patience for those who characterize this as legitimate protest. I know the difference between protest and insurrection—I helped suppress the Whiskey Rebellion in 1794 when armed citizens violently resisted federal law. President Washington's swift, decisive action then preserved the constitutional order. President Trump's inaction on January 6th imperiled it.

The President's defenders argue he called for peaceful protest. Yet his speech was laden with combative language: "We will never give up, we will never concede," "You have to fight like hell," "We're going to walk down to the Capitol." When his supporters did exactly that, violently, he initially did nothing. His eventual video message—"We love you, you're very special"—more endorsement than condemnation.

Consider also his tweet attacking Vice President Pence mid-riot: "Mike Pence didn't have the courage to do what should have been done." This was not a good-faith legal argument—no constitutional scholar of any credibility believed Pence could unilaterally reject electoral votes. This was incitement, directing mob anger toward a man doing his constitutional duty.

The comparison some draw to the American Revolution is offensive. We revolted against a king who gave us no representation in Parliament, no voice in our governance. These rioters sought to nullify an election in which they had every opportunity to vote, to campaign, to persuade—and lost. They had representation; they simply demanded victory.

Energy in the executive is essential to good government, but that energy must serve the Constitution, not personal ambition. Trump's energy on January 6th served only himself.

**This was not patriotism. It was criminal narcissism wrapped in the flag.**

## **James Madison** - *Father of the Constitution, Fourth President*

I must analyze this both systematically and mournfully.

In Federalist #10, I identified faction as the gravest threat to republican government. I defined faction as "a number of citizens...united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community."

January 6th represents faction triumphant—a subset of citizens, animated by passion rather than reason, attempting to impose their will through violence against both the rights of other citizens (whose votes they sought to nullify) and the permanent interests of the community (constitutional order itself).

I designed our system with multiple checks specifically to prevent such dangers:

1. **Separation of Powers**: Congress certifies elections, not the President. The mob sought to disrupt this separation.

2. **Federalism**: States conduct elections under their own laws. The rioters rejected this federal structure, demanding national authority overturn state certifications.

3. **Representative Government**: We do not govern by direct democracy precisely to filter the people's passion through deliberative bodies. The mob sought to bypass this filter entirely.

4. **Rule of Law**: Courts examined every claim of fraud; sixty cases failed. The mob rejected legal adjudication in favor of force.

Every protection I designed was targeted that day.

Most troubling is President Trump's conduct. In Federalist #51, I wrote: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition." Our system assumes leaders will guard their institutional prerogatives. But what happens when a President turns his ambition against his own office, against the very constitutional process that legitimizes all governmental authority?

Trump's refusal to accept electoral defeat represents a systemic failure I had not adequately anticipated: a President who values personal power over institutional integrity, who would rather destroy the process than submit to it.

Some argue he genuinely believed the election was stolen. This is irrelevant. We are a nation of laws, not beliefs. The proper remedy for disputed elections is legal adjudication, which occurred exhaustively. When those legal processes unanimously rejected his claims, his duty was clear: accept the result. Instead, he summoned a mob.

The rioters' actions—breaking windows, assaulting officers, hunting lawmakers—represent precisely the "violence and confusion" I warned would destroy republican government if left unchecked.

Yet I find some hope: The system held. Congress reconvened. The certification proceeded. Vice President Pence honored his constitutional role despite enormous pressure. Federal courts functioned independently. State election officials refused intimidation. The Constitution proved more durable than one man's ambition.

**But the attempt itself has done lasting damage to the people's faith in self-government—and that faith is the only true foundation of our Republic.**

## **Benjamin Franklin** - *Eldest Founder, Diplomat, Sage*

At the Constitutional Convention in 1787, as we concluded our work, a woman asked me: "Well, Doctor, what have we got—a republic or a monarchy?"

I replied: "A republic, if you can keep it."

January 6, 2021, tested whether we could.

I am, by nature, optimistic about human progress. I have seen ordinary people accomplish extraordinary things through reason, cooperation, and compromise. But I have also warned against the dangers of mob rule and demagoguery.

During my lifetime, I witnessed both the noble uprising against British tyranny and the dangerous excesses of revolutionary fervor. I know the difference. The American Revolution was characterized by reasoned argument—we published Common Sense, the Declaration of Independence, petitions to the King. We exhausted peaceful remedies before taking up arms. We sought not personal power but principled liberty.

January 6th bore none of these markers. It was not reasoned argument but violent passion. Not principled resistance but personal loyalty to one man. Not a last resort after exhausted remedies but a first resort to circumvent constitutional processes that had not delivered the desired result.

President Trump's conduct disappoints me particularly. A President must be many things, but above all, he must be truthful with the people. A republic cannot function if leaders systematically deceive citizens about fundamental matters like election integrity. Trump told his supporters for months that their votes were stolen. Sixty courts said otherwise. State officials—including his own party members—said otherwise. His own Attorney General said otherwise.

Yet he persisted in the lie. And when his supporters acted on that lie with violence, he praised them as "very special" people.

This is not leadership. It is manipulation.

I have long believed that "they that can give up essential liberty to obtain a little temporary safety deserve neither liberty nor safety." On January 6th, we saw the inverse: people willing to surrender essential constitutional order to obtain what they perceived as political victory deserve neither order nor victory.

The tragedy is that Trump's supporters genuinely believed they were defending democracy. They were fed falsehoods so consistently and effectively that they could not distinguish patriotism from insurrection. This is the danger of systematic deception by those in authority.

Yet here is my hope: The system worked. The mob did not stop the constitutional process. Vice President Pence fulfilled his duty. Congress certified the election. President Biden took office on schedule. The Republic held.

**But we must learn from this. A republic is maintained not by documents alone but by the character and judgment of its citizens and leaders. January 6th revealed dangerous deficits in both.**

As one who devoted his life to establishing law as the foundation of our Republic, I view January 6th through the lens of legal order versus lawless passion.

I was not at the signing of the Declaration of Independence—I was busy organizing New York's defenses. I did not seek glory but fulfilled duty. This distinction matters: those who truly serve the Republic do so through the system, not despite it.

President Trump's behavior violated multiple legal and constitutional principles:

1. **Presidential Oath**: He swore to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution." His efforts to overturn a lawful election through extralegal pressure and mob violence directly violated this oath.

2. **Separation of Powers**: He pressured Vice President Pence to exceed constitutional authority by rejecting electoral votes—something no Vice President has ever done and no serious constitutional interpretation permits.

3. **Rule of Law**: When sixty courts rejected his fraud claims, his duty was to accept legal adjudication. Instead, he encouraged his supporters to reject judicial authority.

4. **Incitement**: His January 6th speech, followed by hours of inaction as violence unfolded, meets any reasonable definition of inciting and failing to suppress insurrection.

I negotiated the Jay Treaty with Britain in 1794, enduring massive public criticism and even being burned in effigy. I understood then what Trump seems not to grasp: in a republic, you will sometimes be on the losing side of public opinion. The proper response is to persuade, to organize politically, to await the next election. The improper response is to reject the constitutional process itself.

Some claim Trump supporters were exercising free speech. Let me be clear: Free speech is protected. Violence is not. Property destruction is not. Assaulting law enforcement officers is not. Hunting lawmakers through the Capitol is not.

The rioters were not protesters. They were criminals who must face legal accountability.

I am also deeply concerned about the precedent. If a President can lose an election, claim fraud without evidence, summon a mob to prevent certification, and face no meaningful consequences, what prevents future Presidents from attempting the same?

**Republican government requires that ambition check ambition. On January 6th, presidential ambition faced no check until violent insurrection forced it.**

## **John Adams** - *Second President, Political Philosopher*

I have spent my life studying the mechanics and pathologies of republics. January 6th, 2021, represents a textbook case of democratic decay.

In my "Thoughts on Government" (1776), I warned: "There is no good government but what is republican... the very definition of a republic is 'an empire of laws, and not of men.'"

What occurred on January 6th was precisely the opposite: an attempt to establish an empire of one man over the law.

I know something of contested elections—I lost to Jefferson in 1800 in a bitter, divisive campaign where my opponents called me a tyrant and warmonger. I believed Jefferson's presidency would be disastrous for America. But when the electoral votes were counted and Jefferson won, I did my constitutional duty: I departed Washington before his inauguration (admittedly bitter about it), but I departed. The transfer of power proceeded peacefully.

This is not weakness. It is the essence of republican virtue: subordinating personal ambition and grievance to constitutional order.

Trump's refusal to accept defeat is not just poor sportsmanship—it strikes at the foundation of democratic government. If the losing side of an election can simply declare the result illegitimate and use violence to overturn it, then elections become meaningless. We descend into either tyranny or chaos.

I warned in my writings that democracy contains the seeds of its own destruction—that unchecked popular passion can destroy the very liberty it claims to protect. The mob that stormed the Capitol believed themselves patriots defending freedom. In reality, they were tyrants attempting to impose their will through force.

President Trump's defenders argue he faced a biased media, hostile institutions, and unfair treatment. Perhaps some of this is true—I certainly felt the same during my presidency. But the remedy for perceived injustice within the system is not to destroy the system. It is to work harder at persuasion, to organize more effectively, to win the next election.

Trump had every legal avenue available. He used them. He lost. Sixty courts—including judges he appointed—rejected his claims. This is not evidence of a rigged system but of a functioning one.

What troubles me most is Trump's appeal to his supporters' passion rather than their reason. Demagoguery—the art of manipulating popular emotion for personal power—is the oldest threat to republican government. I saw it in ancient Athens, in revolutionary France, in my own time. January 6th was American demagoguery incarnate.

**John Adams on his death would famously say: "Thomas Jefferson survives." (He was wrong—Jefferson had died hours earlier.)** 

**If I were to speak from beyond on January 6th, I would say: "The Constitution survives." And thank Providence it did.**

## Areas of Consensus Among the Council

Despite their different temperaments and political philosophies, the Founding Fathers would find remarkable agreement on January 6th:

1. **Presidential Duty Failed**: Trump violated his oath to preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution by inciting and failing to suppress mob violence against constitutional processes.

2. **Not Legitimate Protest**: The violence was not comparable to the American Revolution. The colonists had no representation; Trump's supporters had lost an election where they had every opportunity to participate.

3. **Legal Remedies Were Available**: Sixty court cases, multiple recounts, state certifications, and congressional objection procedures—all were used and found Trump's claims without merit.

4. **Vice President Pence Acted Honorably**: He resisted unconstitutional pressure and fulfilled his ceremonial duty, exemplifying the principle that institutional integrity must prevail over factional loyalty.

5. **The System Held—Barely**: While the Constitution proved resilient, the incident revealed dangerous vulnerabilities in assuming presidential good faith.

6. **Precedent Matters Gravely**: Future demagogues may attempt similar tactics. Swift legal and political accountability is essential to deter repetition.

7. **Faction Unchecked Is Dangerous**: The mob represented precisely the kind of passionate faction the Constitution was designed to control through structural checks and balances.

## Areas of Disagreement

**Jefferson** would be most sympathetic to the grievances (though not the methods), believing that periodic political upheaval, even if misguided, reflects democratic vitality.

**Hamilton** would be harshest on Trump personally, seeing him as the exact type of demagogue he warned against—beginning as a populist and revealed as an aspiring tyrant.

**Washington** would focus on the damage to national unity and the precedent of violence in political disputes.

**Madison** would emphasize systematic analysis of how constitutional safeguards functioned and where they need reinforcement.

**Franklin** would temper criticism with hope, noting that the system ultimately held and urging focus on civic education to prevent recurrence.

**Adams and Jay** would stress legal accountability and the imperative that no one—especially the President—be above the law.
