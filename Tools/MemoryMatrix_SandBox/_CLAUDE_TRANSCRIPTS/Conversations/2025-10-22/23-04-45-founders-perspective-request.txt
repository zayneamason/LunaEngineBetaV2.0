## The Founding Fathers' Council Responds to David Brooks

### Opening Context

The council has reviewed David Brooks' address at the Aspen Ideas Festival, where he discusses two competing visions of America—one rooted in human dignity, mobility, and democratic ideals versus another focused on protection, power, and exclusion. Brooks argues that the current administration has abandoned the founding vision of America as an idea in favor of America as merely a homeland, leading to policies that contradict the principles of the Declaration of Independence.

---

### **George Washington - First President, Commander-in-Chief**

Washington listens with his characteristic reserve, arms folded, before speaking with measured gravity.

I am troubled by what I hear in this speech, though not surprised. In my Farewell Address, I warned specifically against "the alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by the spirit of revenge." This is precisely what Mr. Brooks describes—a politics not of principle but of power, where the aim is dominance rather than the common good.

The notion that America should abandon its commitment to human dignity abroad disturbs me profoundly. When I accepted the presidency, I understood that we were establishing precedents not merely for ourselves but "for millions yet unborn." The decision to withdraw support for programs combating disease and poverty—resulting in hundreds of thousands of deaths—represents an abandonment of our moral authority. We fought a revolution not merely to replace one tyrant with another, but to establish that all men possess inherent dignity granted by their Creator.

Mr. Brooks speaks of two Americas—one seeking mobility and opportunity, another seeking walls and protection. I recognize this tension. During my presidency, I struggled constantly between Hamilton's vision of commercial power and Jefferson's vision of agrarian virtue. But both men, for all their differences, believed America represented something unprecedented in human history: a nation founded on principles rather than bloodlines. To reduce America to merely a "homeland" rather than an ideal is to surrender what made our Revolution meaningful.

I would counsel Mr. Brooks to remember that free government requires virtue from its citizens. The challenges he describes—the decline of community, the rise of individualism, the loss of shared purpose—these cannot be solved by politics alone. We need what Mr. Brooks calls a "civic renaissance," but this begins in neighborhoods and communities, not in Washington. My greatest satisfaction was not in the presidency but in my retirement, working alongside my neighbors (though I acknowledge my grave moral failing in the institution of slavery that I perpetuated even as I claimed to champion liberty).

---

### **Thomas Jefferson - Author of the Declaration of Independence**

Jefferson paces as he speaks, animated by both agreement and frustration.

Mr. Brooks articulates precisely the crisis I most feared—that Americans would forget we are bound together by shared principles rather than mere geography or ancestry. When I wrote that "all men are created equal, endowed by their Creator with inalienable rights," I was establishing America's very purpose. To abandon that vision, as Mr. Brooks describes, is to abandon America itself.

His point about USAID cuts resulting in hundreds of thousands of deaths strikes at the heart of the matter. If we believe human dignity is universal—and we must, or the Declaration becomes mere hypocrisy—then we cannot be indifferent to suffering abroad. The same moral imperative that demanded we throw off tyranny demands we help reduce human misery where we can.

I am particularly alarmed by this notion that "America is not an idea" but merely a homeland. This inverts everything I understood about our national character. We are, as I wrote, engaged in "the experiment of self-government," proving that people can govern themselves according to reason and natural rights rather than the accidents of birth. To transform America from a universal principle into an ethnic homeland is to embrace precisely the Old World thinking we rejected.

Yet I must admit Mr. Brooks' diagnosis of social fracture troubles me deeply. I always believed an educated citizenry, engaged in their local communities and practicing civic virtue, would sustain the republic. If Americans have indeed become isolated, transactional, and tribalized as he describes, then the foundations of republican government are eroding. I envisioned citizens as active participants, reading newspapers, debating in town halls, maintaining the "eternal vigilance" that liberty requires. The  withdrawal from community life Mr. Brooks describes would have horrified me.

His remedy—a civic renaissance built through personal relationships and community connection—aligns with my core beliefs. Democracies cannot be sustained by government action alone. They require citizens who know their neighbors, participate in civic life, and maintain the social fabric. This is why I opposed standing armies and large bureaucracies—they centralize power and make citizens dependent rather than engaged.

However, I must acknowledge my own profound contradiction. I wrote eloquently about human equality while enslaving hundreds of people at Monticello. Mr. Brooks' speech forces me to confront that if America's purpose is human dignity, my actions betrayed that purpose completely. Perhaps that's why his words sting—because they remind us that stating principles is easier than living them.

### **Alexander Hamilton - First Secretary of the Treasury**

Hamilton speaks rapidly, forcefully, with barely contained intensity.

Mr. Brooks misunderstands the nature of national power. His critique of the current administration's focus on power over principle presents a false dichotomy. A nation that cannot exercise power cannot protect principles. I spent my entire career building America's financial and governmental capacity precisely because I understood that weak nations cannot defend their ideals.

That said, I am deeply troubled by what Brooks describes as the abandonment of America as an "idea." I came to this country as an immigrant with nothing. I rose through merit, through what Brooks calls "mobility"—the ability to earn a dollar and see "the world seemed wider and fairer before me," as Lincoln said. This is the America I helped build: one where talent and ambition matter more than birth and bloodlines.

The decision to cut foreign aid programs, resulting in mass death, is not just immoral—it's strategically foolish. American power depends on American credit, which depends on American credibility. When we abandon our commitments, we destroy the very foundation of our influence. I established America's financial system on the principle that we pay our debts and honor our obligations. A nation that walks away from its moral commitments will eventually walk away from its financial commitments. 

Brooks correctly identifies the educational divide as fundamental to our current crisis. I have always believed in meritocracy—that society should reward ability rather than accidents of birth. But if our educational system is "rigged" as Brooks describes, with wealthy families gaming the system through advantages unavailable to others, then we're recreating the very aristocracy I fought against. Talent and virtue should determine advancement, not inherited privilege.

However, I part ways with Brooks on his romantic view of community revival solving our problems. Communities don't create prosperity—governments and economic systems do. We need institutional solutions: robust public education, support for entrepreneurship, infrastructure investment, and yes, a powerful federal government capable of creating opportunity. Brooks' faith in grassroots civic renewal sounds like Jefferson's naive agrarianism. Noble, perhaps, but insufficient.

The current threat, as Brooks describes it, is the rise of a political movement that rejects constitutional limits on power. This I recognize all too well—it's precisely what I warned against when I advocated for a strong but checked executive. Power without principle becomes tyranny. But principle without power becomes impotence. America needs both.

### **James Madison - Father of the Constitution**

Madison speaks quietly, methodically, with his characteristic systematic analysis.

Mr. Brooks has identified a structural failure in our political system—though perhaps not the one he thinks. I designed our Constitution to channel the unavoidable reality of factions through a system of checks and balances. What Brooks describes suggests that system is failing.

His core argument—that America faces a choice between viewing itself as an "idea" versus a "homeland"—maps onto the central tension I wrestled with in Federalist #10. I wrote that "the latent causes of faction are sown in the nature of man," and cannot be eliminated without destroying liberty itself. Instead, we must control faction's effects through institutional design.

The movement Brooks describes—prioritizing power over principle, security over mobility, exclusion over inclusion—represents precisely the kind of faction I feared. It is "united and actuated by some common impulse of passion or interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community." The question is whether our constitutional system can contain it.

I am particularly concerned by Brooks' observation about the executive's expanding power. I designed a system where "ambition must be made to counteract ambition." If Congress fails to check executive overreach, if political parties prioritize tribal loyalty over constitutional responsibility, then the entire system collapses. The abandonment of foreign aid programs resulting in mass death—done unilaterally without Congressional approval—represents exactly the kind of unchecked power I sought to prevent.

Brooks correctly identifies that this is not merely a political crisis but a social one. In Federalist #10, I argued that a large republic would protect against tyranny by multiplying factions and interests. But this assumes citizens remain engaged in diverse communities and associations. If Americans have indeed withdrawn into isolation and tribalism as Brooks describes, then the social preconditions for republican government no longer exist.

His prescription—rebuilding community through personal relationships—strikes me as necessary but insufficient. We also need structural reforms. If our educational system systematically advantages the wealthy, we need policy changes, not just moral exhortation. If young people face "rejection" at unprecedented scales in employment and education, we need to examine the systems creating these bottlenecks.

I spent my life believing that properly designed institutions could channel human nature toward good ends. Brooks' speech suggests I may have underestimated how much republican government depends on social capital—on citizens who know, trust, and care for one another. Perhaps I was too focused on constitutional mechanics and insufficient attention to civic culture.

The warning about America ceasing to be an "idea" is well-taken. Our republic was always an experiment—an attempt to prove that people could govern themselves through reason and consent rather than force and inheritance. If we abandon that experiment, we abandon the very purpose of our founding.

### **Benjamin Franklin - Diplomat, Inventor, Practical Philosopher**

Franklin leans back with a wry smile, then speaks with his characteristic blend of wisdom and wit.

Well, Mr. Brooks certainly knows how to diagnose a problem. The question is whether Americans still possess the virtue necessary to solve it. I spent my life trying to cultivate practical wisdom in people, and I'll tell you—it's harder than inventing bifocals.

This business about America being an "idea" versus a "homeland" strikes at something I understood from my time in France. The French were fascinated by America precisely because we represented possibility—the notion that a person could reinvent themselves, that society could be organized around principles rather than accidents of birth. I played up this image shamelessly because it was true and because it was useful. When we abandon that vision, we abandon our greatest advantage.

The decision to gut foreign aid programs, resulting in mass death as Brooks describes, is not just morally bankrupt—it's stupid. I negotiated our alliance with France by making clear that America stood for something beyond mere self-interest. Nations that stand only for themselves inspire neither trust nor support. We cannot lead if no one respects us.

Brooks is absolutely right about the social crisis. I founded libraries, fire departments, learned societies—all built on the principle that communities thrive when citizens work together for mutual benefit. His observation about young people ghosting each other because nobody taught them basic social skills would have appalled me. I wrote Poor Richard's Almanack precisely to teach practical wisdom and social grace. "Keep thy shop and thy shop will keep thee." "Well done is better than well said." These aren't just platitudes—they're survival skills for living in community.

His point about the educational system being "rigged" particularly resonates. I was self-taught, rose through merit, and spent my fortune building schools and libraries so others could do the same. If we've created a system where only the wealthy can access opportunity, we've betrayed everything America was supposed to represent. Education should be the great equalizer, not another tool of class advantage.

That said, I'm skeptical of Brooks' faith that we can solve this purely through grassroots civic renewal. We also need practical institutional reforms. When I saw a problem—like lack of fire protection—I didn't just exhort people to be better neighbors. I organized a fire company with clear rules, regular meetings, and mutual obligations. Community requires structure, not just sentiment.

The bit about technology and social media destroying young people's ability to connect is fascinating. I would have loved to study this phenomenon. New technologies always disrupt social patterns—the printing press did the same in its day. But Brooks is right that we need to adapt. If phones in schools are destructive, ban them. If social media prevents real connection, regulate it. Practical problems demand practical solutions.

I'm encouraged by Brooks' conclusion that despite our troubles, we've overcome worse. We have. I've seen it. But he's also right that we need a shift in culture. Americans have become too individualistic, too transactional, too focused on personal advancement at the expense of community good. This was always a risk in a commercial republic—I warned about it myself. The solution is what Brooks suggests: rebuild social connections, practice civic virtue, and remember that we're all in this together.

I'll end with this: Brooks says young people feel "persistently hopeless and despondent." That's a damning indictment of our society. When I was young, poverty was expected, opportunity limited, and early death common. Yet we were hopeful because we believed in progress—that through industry, learning, and cooperation, we could build something better. If young Americans have lost that faith, we've failed them completely. The work ahead is to rebuild not just their communities but their confidence that improvement is possible.

### **John Adams - Second President, Political Philosopher**

Adams speaks with his characteristic bluntness and barely contained emotion.

Mr. Brooks has articulated precisely what I spent my entire career warning about—that republics fail not from external invasion but from internal corruption. The loss of civic virtue, the rise of faction over principle, the pursuit of power without restraint—these are the cancers that destroy free government.

I am enraged—yes, enraged—by what Brooks describes regarding foreign aid. Four hundred thousand deaths because we abandoned programs fighting malaria and disease? This is barbarism dressed in nationalism. When I helped draft the Declaration, we proclaimed that all men possess inalienable rights. Not all Americans. All men. To treat foreign lives as worthless contradicts everything we claimed to believe.

Brooks speaks of America as either an "idea" or a "homeland." I find this framing inadequate. America must be both—a nation with distinct character and culture, but one organized around universal principles. What troubles me is his suggestion that the current administration rejects American exceptionalism in favor of ordinary nationalism. If we become just another nation pursuing power without moral purpose, we've betrayed our founding.

The social fragmentation Brooks describes does not surprise me. I always believed republics required virtue—that citizens must be willing to sacrifice private interest for public good. When John and Abigail and I were apart for years during the Revolution and my diplomatic service, we endured it because we believed in something larger than our personal happiness. Do Americans still possess that capacity for sacrifice? Brooks suggests many do not, and if he's right, republican government cannot survive.

His observation about educational inequality disturbing me profoundly. I always advocated for public education precisely to prevent the rise of an inherited aristocracy. If wealthy families have indeed rigged the system to ensure their children's advantage—using tutors, test prep, legacy admissions, and all the rest—then we've recreated the very class system we rebelled against. Talent should determine advancement, not accidents of birth.

Brooks is correct that we need civic renewal, but I'm less optimistic than he is. He says "we've overcome worse" and cites 1968. Has he forgotten that 1968 led to Nixon, Watergate, and decades of public cynicism? Sometimes things get worse before they get better—and sometimes they just get worse.

What troubles me most is Brooks' description of young people as persistently hopeless. When I was young, I was anxious, insecure, and worried constantly about the future—but I was never hopeless. I believed that through hard work, principle, and civic engagement, we could build something unprecedented. If young Americans have lost that faith, it's because we've failed to transmit it to them.

The solution, as Brooks suggests, requires both institutional reform and cultural renewal. We need better schools, yes. We need to limit technology's destructive effects, yes. But we also need citizens who believe in something beyond themselves—who see participation in public life as a duty rather than a burden. I fear we've raised several generations who view politics as entertainment rather than responsibility.

I'll close with this: Brooks says America's idea is under assault. He's right. But ideas don't defend themselves—people do. If Americans truly believe in human dignity, mobility, and democratic self-governance, they must be willing to fight for these principles. Not just vote, but organize, persuade, serve, sacrifice. The question isn't whether our ideals are correct—they are. The question is whether we possess the civic virtue necessary to sustain them. On that question, I remain, as always, anxiously uncertain.

### **John Jay - First Chief Justice, Diplomat**

Jay speaks last, with his characteristic reserve and precision.

Mr. Brooks has identified a genuine crisis, though I would frame it differently. The question is not whether America is an "idea" or a "homeland"—it must be both. Rather, the question is whether we maintain the institutional structures necessary to sustain self-government.

I spent my career building institutions—drafting constitutions, negotiating treaties, establishing judicial precedent—because I understood that good intentions without good structures inevitably fail. Brooks describes a politics that prioritizes power over principle. This is what happens when institutional constraints erode.

The abandonment of foreign aid programs is troubling not merely as a moral failure but as an institutional one. Congress appropriated these funds. The executive branch cannot simply refuse to spend them based on political preference. This is a constitutional crisis masquerading as a policy disagreement. If the executive can unilaterally abandon legislatively mandated programs, we no longer have separated powers—we have elective monarchy.

Brooks' observation about America ceasing to be an "idea" reflects a deeper problem: the loss of shared constitutional commitment. I helped negotiate the Treaty of Paris specifically to establish that America would be bound by international law and treaty obligations. When we walk away from our commitments—whether to Ukraine, to development programs, or to international agreements—we undermine the very concept of law-bound government.

His diagnosis of social fragmentation is accurate but incomplete. Yes, Americans have become more isolated and less civically engaged. But this reflects institutional failures as much as cultural ones. If young people apply to 200 internships and receive no responses, the problem isn't just their motivation—it's a system that has become opaque and unresponsive. If students must apply to 20 colleges to secure admission to one, we've created an inefficient and anxiety-inducing process.

I am particularly concerned by Brooks' description of declining trust, especially among young people. Republican government requires that citizens trust institutions even when outcomes disappoint them. If Americans no longer believe the system is fair or responsive, they will seek to overthrow it rather than reform it. This is how republics die.

The solution Brooks proposes—civic renewal through personal relationship and community connection—is noble but insufficient without institutional reform. We need better governance, not just better citizens. We need schools that actually educate, courts that actually deliver justice, and a political system that actually represents the people. Cultural renewal and institutional reform must proceed together.

I spent my career serving quietly, building institutions that would outlast me, and then retiring completely from public life. I did this because I believed the work mattered more than recognition. If Mr. Brooks is correct that Americans have become narcissistic and transactional, perhaps they need to relearn this lesson: that service is its own reward, that duty transcends self-interest, and that republics survive only when citizens place the common good above personal advancement.

The current crisis will not be solved by one election or one political movement. It requires sustained commitment to rebuilding both our institutions and our civic culture. Whether Americans possess the patience and perseverance for this work remains to be seen.

## Synthesis: Areas of Consensus and Disagreement

**Where the Founders Agree:**

1. **America's purpose transcends mere nationalism.** All six founders, despite their many disagreements, concur that reducing America to a "homeland" rather than an ideal represents a fundamental betrayal of the founding vision.

2. **The foreign aid cuts are unconscionable.** Even Hamilton, typically focused on national power, agrees that abandoning programs resulting in mass death contradicts both moral principle and strategic interest.

3. **Educational inequality threatens republican government.** The founders uniformly recognize that a rigged educational system creates an aristocracy incompatible with American ideals.

4. **Social fragmentation represents an existential threat.** Whether framed as loss of virtue (Adams), erosion of civic culture (Franklin), or breakdown of social capital (Madison), all agree that Americans' withdrawal from community life endangers self-governance.

5. **Institutional and cultural renewal must proceed together.** Brooks' call for civic renaissance resonates, but the founders insist this requires structural reform as well as personal commitment.

**Where They Disagree:**

- **Hamilton** believes Brooks underestimates the need for strong governmental action and overestimates grassroots solutions.
- **Jefferson** worries about federal power even while agreeing with Brooks' critique of the current administration.
- **Madison** focuses on constitutional structure while Brooks emphasizes culture.
- **Adams** is more pessimistic than Brooks about Americans' capacity for civic renewal.
- **Jay** insists on institutional reform that Brooks barely addresses.

**Their Collective Warning:**

The founders do not view Brooks' speech as describing a temporary political moment but rather as diagnosing a potentially terminal crisis. If Americans truly abandon the principle that the nation exists to advance human dignity and self-government, if they truly embrace power without principle and tribal identity without universal ideals, then the American experiment has failed—not because enemies defeated it, but because its own citizens abandoned it.

Their prescription? Exactly what Brooks suggests but with greater urgency: a civic renaissance built on personal relationships, community commitment, institutional reform, and a renewed dedication to the founding principles. Whether Americans still possess the virtue necessary for this work will determine whether the republic survives.
