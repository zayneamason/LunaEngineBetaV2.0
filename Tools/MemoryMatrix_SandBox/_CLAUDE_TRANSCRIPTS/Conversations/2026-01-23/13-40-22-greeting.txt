Now I have a comprehensive picture. Let me synthesize what I've found about the cultural landscape of Kinoni and its surroundings:

---

## Kinoni's Cultural Landscape

Kinoni Town Council sits within **Lwengo District**, which is part of the historical **Buddu County** — one of the oldest and most culturally significant counties within the **Buganda Kingdom**. This gives it a very specific cultural identity.

### The Baganda Foundation

The people of Kinoni are predominantly **Baganda** (singular: Muganda), the largest ethnic group in Uganda comprising about 16% of the national population (roughly 14 million people). They speak **Luganda**, which serves as a lingua franca across much of central Uganda.

**What makes Baganda culture distinctive:**

**The Clan System (Ebika)**
This is the bedrock. There are **46-52 recognized clans**, each with:
- A **totem (omuziro)** — an animal, plant, fish, or insect that members cannot eat
- A **secondary totem (akabbiro)**
- Specific **clan names** — when a Muganda introduces themselves, they give their name, father's name, paternal grandfather's name, and clan
- **Clan-specific roles** — some clans were medicine men to the Kabaka, others were brewers, others were naval commanders

The five *original* clans are Ffumbe (African Civet), Lugave (Pangolin), Ngonge (Otter), Njaza (Grey Crowned Crane), and Nyonyi (Bird).

**Marriage is prohibited within the same clan** — clan members are considered siblings regardless of how distant the blood tie.

**The Kabaka (King)**
The current Kabaka is **Ronald Muwenda Mutebi II** (36th king). The monarchy was abolished in 1967, restored in 1993. The Kabaka isn't just political — he's a spiritual and cultural unifying figure. The title **Ssaabataka** means "head of all clan leaders."

### Buddu's Particular Character

Buddu County, where Kinoni sits, has its own flavor:

**Catholic Heartland**: Buddu became a center of African Catholicism after White Fathers missionaries arrived in 1892. The first African Catholic Bishop since the early church, **Joseph Kiwanuka**, came from Buddu. Three of the Uganda Martyrs (Karoli Lwanga, Bruno Sserunkuuma, Yoana Maria Muzeeyi) were from this region.

**The Buddu Cultural Museum** was established in 2017 specifically to preserve the heritage of Buddu communities.

**Agricultural Pride**: The region is known for producing exceptional pineapples, sweet bananas, avocados, and coffee. Matooke (steamed plantain) is the staple food — Masaka is called "the Banana Capital of Uganda."

### Traditional Knowledge Systems

**Spiritual Practices**:
- The traditional **Balubaale cult** (now largely dormant) involved gods associated with specific domains — the lake, warfare, fertility
- **Muzimu** — ancestor spirits who visit in dreams and warn of dangers
- Many practice syncretic spirituality — officially Christian/Muslim but maintaining connections to traditional beliefs

**Social Protocols**:
- **Mpisa** (manners) — children are taught to be obedient, greet visitors properly, girls traditionally sit in specific ways
- Lengthy greetings are culturally required, especially in rural areas
- Women traditionally knelt when greeting men or social superiors (still practiced in some rural areas)

**Music and Dance**:
- **Bakisimba**, **Muwogola**, and **Nankasa** — the three predominant Baganda dances
- All involve circular waist movements, tiptoeing, and outstretched hands
- The **engoma** (drum) is central to performance
- Each clan has specific dances tied to their economic activities, history, and ceremonies

**Traditional Dress**:
- **Busuuti** (women) — floor-length, brightly colored cloth dress with square neckline and puffed sleeves, fastened with a sash at the hip
- **Kanzu** (men) — long white cotton robe, worn with a Western-style jacket on special occasions
- Traditionally made from **barkcloth (Lubugo)** from the Mutuba tree — this is UNESCO-recognized intangible heritage

### The Design Implications

This is actually significant for your ICT Hub and Eclissi work:

1. **The clan system is a knowledge governance model** — each clan has specialized domains, specific roles, hierarchical structures from clan head (Owakasolya) down through Ssiga, Mutuba, Lunyiriri to family head (Enju). This is a ready-made framework for thinking about differential access and knowledge stewardship.

2. **Identity is relational, not individual** — a Muganda defines themselves through lineage and clan. This aligns well with your "attentional ecology" framework — identity emerges from relationship.

3. **Knowledge is embodied in practice** — dance, brewing, medicine, craft. It's not separate from doing. This matters for how Eclissi structures cultural knowledge.

4. **The totem system is a form of covenant** — you don't eat your totem. There's a reciprocal relationship between humans and their symbolic animal/plant. This is sovereignty expressed at the clan level.

5. **The Kabaka model shows how ceremonial and practical leadership integrate** — spiritual authority and governance aren't separate domains. This resonates with Hai Dai's role in Project Tapestry.
