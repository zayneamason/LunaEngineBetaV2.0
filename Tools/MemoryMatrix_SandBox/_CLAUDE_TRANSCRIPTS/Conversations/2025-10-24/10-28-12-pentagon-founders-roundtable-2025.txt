## **George Washington - First President, Commander-in-Chief**

**His Reaction: Profound Distress at the Degradation of Executive Dignity**

Washington, who reluctantly accepted power and voluntarily relinquished it, valued duty, dignity, and restraint above all else. He believed the presidency required a measured tone and careful consideration of posterity.

**What would appall him:**

The *tone*. Washington carefully crafted his public addresses to be formal, measured, and focused on national unity. This speech careens between policy discussion, personal grievances, insults of named individuals ("low IQ person"), campaign-style boasting, and casual asides. Washington would see this as beneath the office.

The *self-promotion*. Lines like "we have the highest stock market... greatest economy in our history... 20 trillion dollars of investment" would strike Washington as vulgar. He consistently deflected credit and worried about appearing ambitious or self-aggrandizing.

The *partisan warfare*. The repeated attacks on "the Democrats," "Biden," and specific members of Congress directly violate Washington's Farewell Address warnings about "the alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by the spirit of revenge".

The *during a crisis*. Giving this speech during a government shutdown, while attacking the opposition party's intelligence and motives, is precisely the kind of partisan exploitation Washington warned would become "a frightful despotism".

**Washington's likely assessment:**

> "This speech confirms my deepest fears about what the office of President could become in the hands of one who seeks power for its own sake rather than accepting it as an unwanted burden. The President speaks not as the nation's servant but as a faction's champion. He uses a security briefing to advance political attacks. He measures success by personal acclaim rather than the nation's welfare. This is precisely the 'love of fame and popularity' I warned would corrupt our Republic."

## **Benjamin Franklin - Diplomat, Scientist, Pragmatist**

**His Reaction: Bemused Recognition of Political Reality, But Deep Concern About Diplomatic Damage**

Franklin was the most pragmatic and least ideological Founder. He understood human nature, had a sense of humor about politics, and believed in practical results over philosophical purity.

Franklin would read this transcript and chuckle at some parts—he'd recognize the showmanship, the crowd-pleasing, the strategic use of enemies. Franklin himself was a master of public relations and knew how to work an audience. He'd appreciate the political skill, even if he disapproved of the execution.

**What would concern him:**

The *diplomatic implications*. Franklin would immediately note the president calling Colombia "a drug den" with "a lousy leader... thug" and saying Mexico "is run by the cartels." As someone who spent years carefully building relationships with France, Franklin understood that insulting foreign leaders in public speeches has consequences.

The *lack of wit*. Franklin's political attacks were clever, subtle, and often disguised as humor. This speech's insults are blunt instruments: "low IQ," "deranged," "garbage." Franklin would find this crude and counterproductive.

The *tariff claims*. Franklin was a sophisticated economic thinker who understood trade. The claim that "we're the richest nation in the world now because of tariffs" would make him raise an eyebrow. Franklin believed in practical experimentation, but he'd want to see the actual data.

**Franklin's likely assessment:**

> "The president understands how to command a room, I'll grant him that. But he confuses entertainment with governance. Insulting Colombia's president may please this audience, but it complicates actual diplomacy. Calling opponents 'low IQ' may rally supporters, but it precludes compromise. And claiming tariffs alone made us wealthy ignores centuries of economic theory. I appreciate bold experimentation, but I'd like to see the accounting books before accepting such claims. A wise leader knows when to charm and when to govern—this speech never made the transition."

## **Thomas Jefferson - Author of Declaration, Advocate of Limited Government**

**His Reaction: Horrified by Executive Power Expansion and Militarization**

Jefferson was the Founder most suspicious of concentrated power. He believed in strict limits on federal authority, feared standing armies, and worried constantly about executive overreach.

This speech would trigger every Jeffersonian alarm bell simultaneously.

**What would horrify him:**

The *designation of cartels as "foreign terrorist organizations"* and creation of a "Homeland Security Task Force" combining "Department of Justice, Department of War, DHS, intelligence community, ATF, DEA, US Marshals, and state and local agencies." Jefferson believed power should be dispersed, not concentrated. He specifically feared the military-law enforcement combination.

The *military language*: "We're waging war on them like they've never seen before." Jefferson was extremely wary of using military force domestically and of permanent war footing.

The *120,000 arrests* being celebrated. Jefferson would immediately ask: Under what legal authority? With what due process protections? He believed individual liberty required strict limits on government police power.

The *economic nationalism*. "Thank God for tariffs. If we didn't have tariffs, we'd be a third world nation." Jefferson opposed Hamilton's manufacturing-focused economic vision and believed in an agricultural republic with limited federal economic intervention.

**Jefferson's likely assessment:**

> "This speech describes a consolidation of federal power that would have justified our Revolution against Britain. A 'task force' combining military, intelligence, and law enforcement? Mass arrests celebrated without mention of trials? Economic policy centered on federal trade controls? The President speaks of 'eliminating' threats rather than defending rights. He boasts of exercising power, not limiting it. Every principle we fought for—dispersed authority, individual liberty, civilian restraint, limited government—is violated here. And perhaps most dangerous, he presents all this as necessary to our security, the eternal tyrant's justification."

## **Alexander Hamilton - First Treasury Secretary, Advocate of Strong Federal Power**

**His Reaction: Appreciation for Federal Action, But Alarm at Fiscal Irresponsibility and Incompetence**

Hamilton believed in energetic federal government, strong executive action, and using federal power to solve national problems. He would be the Founder most sympathetic to aggressive federal law enforcement operations.

**What he'd approve:**

The *federal coordination*. Hamilton spent his career arguing that weak federal government couldn't protect the nation. He'd appreciate seeing federal agencies working together on a national security threat.

The *aggressive action*. Hamilton wasn't squeamish about using federal power. He led military forces to suppress the Whiskey Rebellion. He'd support strong action against transnational criminal organizations.

The *executive leadership*. Hamilton believed in a powerful presidency that could act decisively. He'd prefer this to federal paralysis.

**What would appall him:**

The *government shutdown*. Hamilton would be absolutely furious. He built America's financial system on the principle that government must pay its debts and maintain operations. He understood that national credit depends on reliability. The president boasting about achievements while the government isn't functioning would strike Hamilton as fiscal madness.

The *fiscal claims*. "We will have by the end of my first year more than 20 trillion dollars of investment." Hamilton was meticulous about economic data and accounting. He'd demand to see the methodology behind this claim.

The *tariff logic*. "Thank God for tariffs... we're the richest nation in the world now because of tariffs." While Hamilton supported some tariffs for industrial development, he'd recognize this as economically simplistic. Tariffs are one tool, not the foundation of all wealth.

The *incompetence during crisis*. Hamilton handled multiple national crises with systematic planning and execution. This speech during a shutdown, attacking opponents instead of solving the problem, would strike him as amateur hour.

**Hamilton's likely assessment:**

> "The federal coordination against cartels is sound policy—I applaud it. But everything else about this performance is chaos masquerading as leadership. You cannot run a government by shutdown, govern by insult, or measure success by stock prices. The president boasts of $20 trillion in investment while federal workers go unpaid? He celebrates tariffs as our salvation while showing no understanding of how trade actually works? And most damning: he uses a security briefing for political theater. I built systems to work regardless of who held office. This speech shows government has become personality, not institution. That's a recipe for collapse."

## **James Madison - Father of the Constitution, Architect of Checks and Balances**

**His Reaction: Constitutional Crisis and Systematic Breakdown of Republican Government**

Madison designed the Constitution with one overriding goal: prevent concentration of power through institutional checks and balances. He believed faction was natural but must be controlled through structure, not eliminated through force.

Reading this transcript, Madison would see his entire constitutional architecture breaking down.

**What would alarm him:**

The *fusion of powers*. The President speaking at the Pentagon, describing coordination of military ("Department of War"), law enforcement (FBI, DEA, ATF, US Marshals), and intelligence community for domestic operations. Madison specifically designed separation of powers to prevent exactly this kind of consolidated force.

The *partisan governance during crisis*. Madison would note the president attacking the opposition party by name during a government shutdown, while that same opposition's cooperation is needed to resolve it. His Federalist No. 10 warned that faction becomes destructive when it gains control of government machinery and uses it against opposing factions.

The *rhetoric of elimination*. "We're not mitigating, we're eliminating." Madison understood that republican government requires accepting permanent disagreement and opposition. Language of "elimination" of political enemies was exactly what he feared.

The *personalization of government*. The repeated "I" statements: "I signed," "I designated," "I promised," "my administration." Madison designed government to function through institutions, not personality. The Constitution's genius was making government work despite human nature, not depend on individual greatness.

**The breakdown of deliberation**. Madison would immediately notice: Where is Congress in this? The President announces policy, brags about executive actions, attacks legislators—but where is the legislative role? Madison believed the legislature should be primary, with the executive implementing their decisions.

**Madison's likely assessment:**

> "This speech reveals how completely the constitutional structure has failed its purpose. The executive has absorbed legislative and military power. Faction has captured government machinery and wields it against opponents. Crisis is exploited for partisan advantage rather than resolved through deliberation. The president speaks as if government is his personal instrument, not a trust held temporarily. Every check I designed—legislative oversight, separation of powers, federalism, institutional pride—has been bypassed or overcome. This is not monarchy, which we could at least understand. This is something worse: plebiscitary authoritarianism wrapped in republican forms. The Constitution remains, but its spirit is dead."

## **John Adams - Second President, Advocate of Rule of Law**

**His Reaction: Fury at Lawlessness and Degradation of Truth**

Adams was perhaps the most principled Founder regarding rule of law. He defended British soldiers after the Boston Massacre because he believed in legal process over popular passion. He worried constantly about demagogues who would manipulate public opinion.

Adams would read this transcript and explode with rage.

**What would enrage him:**

The *celebration of mass arrests without mention of trials*. "120,000 criminal arrests" treated as inherently good, with no discussion of evidence, charges, convictions, or due process. Adams, who literally defended accused criminals to uphold legal principles, would be appalled.

The *personal attacks on named individuals*. Calling a member of Congress "a low IQ person" by name, from the Pentagon, while president. Adams could be harsh in private letters, but he understood that presidential statements carried institutional weight and required restraint.

The *false claims presented as fact*. "We have the highest stock market... greatest economy in our history... more than 20 trillion dollars of investment... we're the richest nation in the world now because of tariffs." Adams was obsessively honest and hated exaggeration. He'd fact-check every claim here and find most wanting.

The *blame-shifting during shutdown*. Attacking the opposition for the shutdown while refusing to negotiate, calling them "deranged" and claiming they "don't care about the people." Adams understood that in a republic, compromise is essential. Using crisis to attack opponents rather than govern would strike him as demagogic.

**Adams's likely assessment:**

> "This man has no conception of what it means to be president. The office requires truth, restraint, respect for law, and commitment to all citizens—not just supporters. He celebrates arrests without trials, attacks citizens by name, claims credit he hasn't earned, and blames others for problems he could solve. And he does all this while wrapping himself in patriotism and law enforcement. This is precisely the demagoguery I warned against—appealing to popular passion, ignoring institutional limits, treating opponents as enemies. Washington established dignity. Jefferson established principle. I established law. This speech respects none of it. It is the sound of republic dying while applauding itself."

## **John Jay - First Chief Justice, Diplomat**

**His Reaction: Quiet Resignation That the Republic He Built Has Failed**

Jay was the Founder who believed in duty, institutional competence, and quiet service. He did the hardest jobs without seeking recognition, then walked away when his work was done.

Jay would read this transcript and feel profound sadness.

**What would sadden him:**

The *diplomatic damage*. Jay spent years carefully building America's international reputation through the Treaty of Paris and Jay's Treaty. He understood that small nations must be cautious with powerful neighbors and that diplomacy requires patience and restraint. This speech casually insults Colombia and Mexico, America's neighbors and trading partners, for domestic political effect.

The *use of law enforcement for politics*. Jay built the federal judiciary to be independent and above politics. He refused to give advisory opinions to Washington because he believed courts must be separate from political branches. This speech uses law enforcement leaders as political props.

The *abandonment of competence*. Jay's entire career was defined by competent administration—doing difficult jobs well without drama. He negotiated impossible treaties, built institutions, governed effectively. This speech is all performance, no governance.

**Jay's likely assessment:**

> "I spent my life building institutions that could outlast individual leaders. I negotiated treaties that would bind future generations. I established judicial independence so courts could check executive power. I believed that if we built well, the republic would endure regardless of who held office. This speech shows I was wrong. The institutions remain, but they've been hollowed out. The forms persist, but the substance is gone. Diplomacy becomes insult. Law enforcement becomes political theater. Crisis becomes opportunity for blame. I have no more to say. The republic I helped build is dead. What remains wears its skin."

## **SYNTHESIS: THE COUNCIL'S COLLECTIVE WISDOM**

### **Areas of Complete Agreement**

Every Founder, despite their significant differences, would agree on these points:

1. **The degradation of presidential dignity is dangerous**. Washington, Adams, Jefferson, Madison, Hamilton, Franklin, and Jay all understood that the presidency required restraint because the office's power made abuse easy.

2. **Using national security for partisan purposes violates republican principles**. Security briefings should inform and unite, not divide and attack.

3. **A government shutdown during which the president blames opponents while boasting of success is governing malpractice**. Even Hamilton, the most pro-executive Founder, would be appalled.

4. **Personal attacks on named citizens from the presidency is beneath the office**. All Founders maintained some standard of presidential communication, even when they despised each other.

5. **The diplomatic damage from insulting allied nations is real and lasting**. Franklin and Jay especially would recognize this as amateur-hour foreign policy.

### **The Key Disagreement**

**Hamilton** would be the most sympathetic to the aggressive federal law enforcement operation described, while **Jefferson** would be the most alarmed by it. This reflects their fundamental disagreement about federal power that shaped early America.

But even Hamilton would be horrified by the execution, the fiscal irresponsibility during shutdown, and the political theater.

### **The Founders' Warning to Us**

If these seven men could speak to us collectively about this speech, they would say:

*"We gave you a republic with separated powers, checks and balances, and institutional constraints precisely because we knew that ambitious men would seek to accumulate power. We designed government to work despite human nature, not depend on individual virtue. What you see in this transcript—executive power wielded without institutional restraint, faction wielded as weapon, crisis exploited for advantage, opponents treated as enemies—is the republic's immune system failing. The Constitution remains, but the spirit that animated it is gone. You can have energy in government or you can have limited government. You can have partisan warfare or you can have effective government. You cannot have all of these things simultaneously and still call it a republic. We are dead, but our warning remains: this path leads to despotism, whether you call it that or not."*
