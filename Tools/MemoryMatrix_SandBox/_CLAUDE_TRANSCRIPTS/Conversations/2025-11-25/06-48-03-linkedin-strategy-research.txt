Game design isn't really about games—it's about systems, feedback loops, and how people move through structured experiences. You learn to think about pacing. You learn that the best guidance doesn't feel like guidance at all. You learn that people will always find ways to interact with your work that you didn't anticipate, and that's usually a feature, not a bug.

What game design really taught me was how to think about experience from the user's perspective rather than the creator's. When you're designing a level, you're not thinking about what looks cool from above—you're thinking about what it feels like to be inside it. What do people notice first? Where do their eyes go? What makes them want to move forward versus hang back and explore? These questions apply to way more than video games.

The other thing game design drills into you is the importance of invisible systems. The best games don't feel designed. They feel natural, like you're just exploring and things are happening. But underneath, there's an enormous amount of intentional structure making that experience possible. Environmental storytelling, sight lines, audio cues, lighting that subtly guides attention—all of it working together so the player feels like they're discovering something rather than being led.

That framework stuck with me. I kept noticing it everywhere: in retail spaces, in museums, in theme parks, in well-designed buildings. The same principles apply. The good ones feel effortless. The bad ones feel like you're fighting against the space.

When you can build what you design, it keeps you honest. You stop drawing things that look good on paper but don't make sense in reality. You start thinking about materials, tolerances, assembly, weight. You develop an intuition for what's actually achievable versus what's just a nice render.

But more than that, building things connects you to the physical reality of how people interact with objects and spaces. There's something you learn from making a hundred small things that you can't learn from designing on a screen. How surfaces feel. How edges catch light. How weight distribution affects perception. The gap between a prototype and a finished piece, and what it takes to close that gap.
