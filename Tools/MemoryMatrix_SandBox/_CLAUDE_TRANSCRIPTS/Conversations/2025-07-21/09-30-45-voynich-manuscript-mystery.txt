## **Complete Knowledge Base: The Voynich Manuscript**

### **Physical Characteristics & Dating**
The Voynich manuscript is an illustrated codex written on vellum that has been carbon-dated to the early 15th century (1404–1438). The manuscript measures 23.5 by 16.2 by 5 cm (9.3 by 6.4 by 2.0 in), with hundreds of vellum pages collected into 18 quires. The total number of pages is around 240, but the exact number depends on how the manuscript's unusual foldouts are counted.

**Carbon Dating Details:**
Four samples were taken from different folios for radiocarbon dating, yielding a mean of 516±18 BP years or 1421±8.5 years AD, with a 95% confidence interval between 1404-1438. However, there's ongoing debate about whether all samples came from the same time period, with one sample (f68) showing different dating results than the other three.

**Materials Analysis:**
The blue paint is ground azurite with traces of cuprite, the white paint is likely egg-white and calcium carbonate, the green paint is copper and copper-chlorine resinate (possibly atacamite), and the red-brown paint is red ochre with hematite and iron sulphide. The pigments used were deemed inexpensive.

### **Language & Script Analysis**

**Statistical Properties:**
Statistical analysis reveals patterns similar to natural languages, with word entropy about 10 bits per word, similar to English or Latin texts. However, Voynichese has much more predictable character sequences with an h2 (second-order conditional entropy) around 2, while natural languages typically have h2 between 3 and 4.

**Key Linguistic Features:**
- Three-part word structure: prefix, root/midfix, and suffix
- Word frequencies conform to Zipfian distribution, supporting linguistic meaning
- A 2020 study identified the manuscript as the work of five scribes
- Information-theoretic analysis shows complex organization compatible with real language sequences

### **Content Sections**
The manuscript contains distinct sections:
1. **Herbal section** - Unknown plants and botanical illustrations

### **Historical Provenance**
The manuscript belonged to Emperor Rudolf II (1576-1612), who purchased it for 600 gold ducats believing it was the work of Roger Bacon. Recent research by Stefan Guzy suggests Rudolf acquired it from physician Carl Widemann in 1597. The ownership chain includes:
- Rudolf II → Jacobus Horcicky de Tepenecz → Johannes Marcus Marci → Athanasius Kircher → Jesuit College → Wilfrid Voynich (1912) → Yale University (1969)

**Recent AI Attempts:**

**Gerard Cheshire Theory (2019):**
Gerard Cheshire claimed the manuscript was written in "proto-Romance" language, but this was widely rejected by scholars. The University of Bristol withdrew its news release after concerns were raised about the validity of the research.

Statistical analysis shows the manuscript "differs from a random sequence of words, being compatible with natural languages" and "limits severely the scope of the hoax hypothesis". Most linguists now argue for treating the document as natural language rather than a medieval hoax, based on statistical evidence of genuine linguistic structure. The manuscript likely originated in northern Italy, possibly the Veneto region near Verona, Padova, and Vicenza.
